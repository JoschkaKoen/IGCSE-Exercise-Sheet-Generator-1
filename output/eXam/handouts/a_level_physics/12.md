## Angles in radians

The **radian** is the angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc whose length equals the radius. For an arc of length $s$ on a circle of radius $r$, the angle in radians is

$$
\theta = \frac{s}{r}.
$$

Radians have no unit (a ratio of lengths). A full circle: $s = 2\pi r$, so $\theta = 2\pi\ \text{rad}$. A half-circle is $\pi\ \text{rad}$; a quarter is $\pi/2\ \text{rad}$.

Conversions: $1\ \text{rad} = 180°/\pi \approx 57.3°$. Set your calculator to **radians** when working with $\omega$, $T$ and angles in this topic; calculator in "degree" mode will give you wrong values for trigonometry of physics quantities.

## Uniform circular motion: angular speed

An object moves in a circle of radius $r$ at constant speed $v$. Define:

- **angular displacement** $\theta$ — the angle (in radians) swept out by the radius from a chosen starting line.
- **angular speed** (or angular velocity) $\omega$ — the rate of change of angular displacement.

For uniform motion, $\omega$ is constant and

$$
\omega = \frac{\theta}{t}.
$$

Unit: $\text{rad s}^{-1}$.

### Period and frequency

If the object completes one revolution ($2\pi\ \text{rad}$) in time $T$ (the **period**), then

$$
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f,
$$

where $f = 1/T$ is the frequency of revolution (Hz).

### Linear and angular speed

In one period $T$ the object travels a distance $2\pi r$ (one full circumference) at constant speed, so

$$
v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = r \omega.
$$

This is the link between linear (tangential) speed $v$ and angular speed $\omega$. At larger radius (for the same angular speed) the linear speed is greater — a child on the edge of a merry-go-round moves faster than one closer to the centre, even though both complete one revolution in the same time.

## Centripetal acceleration

An object moving in a circle at constant speed has a **changing velocity** — its direction changes continuously, even though its magnitude is constant. A changing velocity requires an acceleration. This acceleration is directed **towards the centre of the circle** and is called the **centripetal acceleration**.

### Magnitude

$$
a = \frac{v^{2}}{r} = r\omega^{2}.
$$

The two forms are equivalent because $v = r\omega$ gives $v^{2}/r = (r\omega)^{2}/r = r\omega^{2}$. Pick the form that uses the quantities you have.

The centripetal acceleration is **perpendicular to the velocity** at every instant — never along the direction of motion. (If there were a component along the motion, the speed would change.)

Unit: $\text{m s}^{-2}$, like any acceleration.

## Centripetal force

By Newton's second law, the resultant force on a body in circular motion at constant speed is

$$
F = m a = \frac{m v^{2}}{r} = m r \omega^{2}.
$$

This is the **centripetal force**. It is **always directed towards the centre of the circle** — perpendicular to the instantaneous velocity.

The centripetal force is not a new kind of force — it is the **net** result of the real forces acting on the object (tension, gravity, friction, electric attraction, normal contact force, etc.). When solving a problem, identify which real force(s) provide the centripetal force in the given scenario.

### Sources of the centripetal force in different scenarios

- **Ball on a string** swung in a horizontal circle: tension in the string provides the centripetal force.
- **Car turning a level corner**: friction between tyres and road provides the centripetal force ($F_{\text{friction}} = m v^{2}/r$). If the speed is too high, friction is exceeded and the car skids outwards.
- **Banked corner** (no friction): the horizontal component of the normal contact force provides the centripetal force; $\tan\theta = v^{2}/(rg)$ for the unique banking angle that needs no friction.
- **Planet or satellite orbiting a star/planet**: gravitational attraction provides the centripetal force, $G M m / r^{2} = m v^{2}/r$.
- **Electron in a circular orbit** (Bohr-style model): the electrostatic attraction between the electron and the positive nucleus provides the centripetal force,

$$
\frac{kZe^{2}}{r^{2}} = \frac{m_{e} v^{2}}{r},
$$

where $k = 1/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0})$ and $Z$ is the nuclear charge. Solve for $v$ to find the orbital speed; then $T = 2\pi r/v$ gives the period.

### Vertical circles

When the circle is in a vertical plane, the speed is **not** constant (gravity does work) — but at each instant the **net radial force towards the centre** still equals $m v^{2}/r$. Set this up carefully:

- at the bottom of a vertical loop: tension up, weight down, $T - mg = m v^{2}/r$, so the tension is largest here.
- at the top of a vertical loop: tension and weight both act downwards (towards centre), so $T + mg = m v^{2}/r$ and the tension is smallest. For minimum speed at the top with the string just taut, set $T = 0$: $mg = m v_{\text{min}}^{2}/r$, giving $v_{\text{min}} = \sqrt{gr}$.

The constant-speed assumption ($v = r\omega$ with $\omega$ constant) holds for horizontal circular motion or where the only role of the force is to bend the path (orbits in gravity, charges in magnetic fields). Use it freely there.

## Worked structure for a circular-motion question

1. **Identify the radius** $r$ and pick the relevant variable: $v$ or $\omega$. Use $v = r\omega$ to switch between them.
2. **Calculate the required centripetal acceleration** with $a = v^{2}/r$ or $r\omega^{2}$.
3. **List the real forces** on the object and write a Newton's-second-law equation in the **radial direction** (towards the centre is positive). Set their net radial component equal to $m v^{2}/r$.
4. If the question asks for **period or frequency**: use $\omega = 2\pi/T$, or $T = 2\pi r / v$.
5. Always check the **direction**: the centripetal force points to the centre. The centripetal acceleration points to the centre. The velocity is tangential.
