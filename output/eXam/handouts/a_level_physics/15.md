## The mole

**Amount of substance** is an SI base quantity. Its unit is the **mole** (mol). The mole is one of the seven SI base units, alongside the kilogram, metre, second, ampere and kelvin that you met in Topic 1.

One mole of any substance contains a number of particles equal to the **Avogadro constant**:

$$
N_{\text{A}} = 6.02 \times 10^{23}\ \text{mol}^{-1}.
$$

A "particle" here means whatever you are counting — atoms for a monatomic element like helium, molecules for $\text{O}_{2}$ or $\text{H}_{2}\text{O}$. Always state what you are counting in case of doubt.

If you have $n$ moles of a substance, the number of particles is

$$
N = n N_{\text{A}}.
$$

The **molar mass** $M_{\text{m}}$ is the mass of one mole of a substance (units $\text{kg mol}^{-1}$ or $\text{g mol}^{-1}$). Mass of $n$ moles is $M = n M_{\text{m}}$. Mass of a single particle is $m_{0} = M_{\text{m}} / N_{\text{A}}$.

## Equation of state of an ideal gas

An **ideal gas** is one that obeys $pV \propto T$ exactly, where $T$ is the **thermodynamic** (absolute) temperature.

The full **equation of state** can be written in two equivalent ways:

$$
p V = n R T \qquad\text{or}\qquad p V = N k T.
$$

In these equations:

- $p$ — pressure (Pa).
- $V$ — volume (m³).
- $T$ — **thermodynamic temperature** in kelvin (never °C).
- $n$ — number of moles.
- $N$ — number of molecules.
- $R$ — molar gas constant, $R = 8.31\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\ \text{K}^{-1}$.
- $k$ — Boltzmann constant, $k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$.

The two are related: $N = n N_{\text{A}}$, so

$$
n R T = N k T \quad\Rightarrow\quad k = \frac{R}{N_{\text{A}}}.
$$

The Boltzmann constant is the gas constant **per molecule**, just as $R$ is per mole.

### Using the equation of state

In any problem state the variables you have, identify the unknown, then choose the form that uses the right kind of "amount" (moles → $nRT$; molecules → $NkT$). Always work in SI base units: Pa, m³, K.

If a gas changes from state 1 to state 2 with no change in the **amount** of gas:

$$
\frac{p_{1} V_{1}}{T_{1}} = \frac{p_{2} V_{2}}{T_{2}}.
$$

Special cases:

- **Constant temperature** (Boyle's law): $p V = \text{constant}$, so $p_{1} V_{1} = p_{2} V_{2}$.
- **Constant pressure** (Charles's law): $V / T = \text{constant}$.
- **Constant volume** (Pressure law): $p / T = \text{constant}$.

A common mistake is using °C instead of K — you would get a wrong answer because $pV \propto T$ holds only with $T$ in kelvin.

## Kinetic theory of gases

The kinetic theory explains the macroscopic behaviour of a gas in terms of the random motion of its molecules. You should know the assumptions and the key result.

### Assumptions

For an **ideal gas**:

1. The gas contains a **large number** of identical molecules in continuous **random motion**.
2. The volume of the molecules themselves is **negligible** compared to the volume of the container.
3. The **time of each collision** (between a molecule and the wall, or between molecules) is **negligible** compared to the time between collisions.
4. **Intermolecular forces** are negligible except during collisions (so molecules travel in straight lines between collisions).
5. **Collisions are elastic** — total kinetic energy is conserved (molecules don't lose KE on average).
6. Newton's laws of motion apply.

These assumptions become poor at very high pressure (molecular volume matters) or very low temperature (intermolecular forces matter). Real gases differ from this idealisation in proportion to these factors.

### Pressure of a gas — derivation outline

Consider a gas in a cubic box of side $L$, with $N$ identical molecules, each of mass $m$. Take one molecule moving along the $x$-axis with velocity $u_{1}$.

- **One collision with the right wall**: velocity reverses from $u_{1}$ to $-u_{1}$. Change in momentum: $\Delta p_{x} = -2 m u_{1}$. By Newton's third law, the wall receives a momentum impulse of $+2 m u_{1}$.
- **Time between successive collisions with the right wall**: the molecule must travel to the opposite wall and back, total distance $2L$. So $\Delta t = 2L/u_{1}$.
- **Average force on the right wall from this one molecule**: $F_{1} = \Delta p / \Delta t = (2 m u_{1}) / (2L/u_{1}) = m u_{1}^{2} / L$.
- **Sum over all $N$ molecules**: $F = (m/L)(u_{1}^{2} + u_{2}^{2} + \ldots) = (Nm/L) \langle u_{x}^{2} \rangle$, where $\langle u_{x}^{2} \rangle$ is the **mean square** of the $x$-component of velocity.
- **Pressure**: $p = F/L^{2} = N m \langle u_{x}^{2} \rangle / L^{3} = N m \langle u_{x}^{2} \rangle / V$.

Now extend to 3-D. By symmetry the motion is the same in $x$, $y$, $z$, so $\langle u_{x}^{2} \rangle = \tfrac{1}{3} \langle c^{2} \rangle$ where $\langle c^{2} \rangle$ is the **mean-square speed** ($c^{2} = u_{x}^{2} + u_{y}^{2} + u_{z}^{2}$). Substituting:

$$
\boxed{\, p V = \tfrac{1}{3} N m \langle c^{2} \rangle.\,}
$$

### Root-mean-square speed

$\langle c^{2} \rangle$ is the average of the squared speeds. Its square root is the **root-mean-square (r.m.s.) speed**:

$$
c_{\text{r.m.s.}} = \sqrt{\langle c^{2} \rangle}.
$$

The r.m.s. speed is a useful single measure of how fast the molecules are moving. It is slightly larger than the mean speed because squaring weights large speeds more heavily.

## Average translational kinetic energy

Compare the two expressions for $pV$:

$$
p V = N k T \quad\text{and}\quad p V = \tfrac{1}{3} N m \langle c^{2} \rangle.
$$

Equate the right-hand sides:

$$
N k T = \tfrac{1}{3} N m \langle c^{2} \rangle.
$$

Cancel $N$ and rearrange. Multiply both sides by $\tfrac{3}{2}$:

$$
\tfrac{3}{2} k T = \tfrac{1}{2} m \langle c^{2} \rangle.
$$

The right-hand side is the **average translational kinetic energy** $\langle E_{\text{k}} \rangle$ of a single molecule. So

$$
\langle E_{\text{k}} \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} m \langle c^{2} \rangle = \tfrac{3}{2} k T.
$$

This is one of the most important results in physics: **the average translational kinetic energy of a molecule of an ideal gas depends only on the thermodynamic temperature**, not on the type of gas or its pressure.

### Consequences and uses

- **Doubling the absolute temperature doubles the average translational KE** of each molecule, and so $\langle c^{2} \rangle$ doubles and $c_{\text{r.m.s.}}$ increases by $\sqrt{2}$.
- For **two different gases at the same temperature**, the lighter gas has a larger $\langle c^{2} \rangle$ (since $\tfrac{1}{2} m \langle c^{2} \rangle$ is fixed). Hydrogen molecules move faster on average than oxygen molecules in the same room.
- **Total translational KE** of $N$ molecules: $\tfrac{3}{2} N k T = \tfrac{3}{2} n R T$.

### Internal energy of an ideal gas

For an **ideal gas**, the molecules are treated as point particles with no internal structure — there is no intermolecular potential energy (assumption 4) and no rotational/vibrational energy in the simple model. The total internal energy is therefore just the total kinetic energy:

$$
U = \tfrac{3}{2} N k T = \tfrac{3}{2} n R T.
$$

This shows directly that **the internal energy of an ideal gas is proportional to the thermodynamic temperature**. Doubling $T$ doubles $U$. (For real gases this is only approximately true, and additional energy can be stored in molecular rotation and vibration.)

### Effect of changing pressure at fixed temperature

Doubling $p$ at fixed $T$ by squeezing the gas to half its volume does **not** change $\langle E_{\text{k}} \rangle$ — that depends only on $T$. The number of collisions with the walls per second increases, but each molecule has the same average kinetic energy.
