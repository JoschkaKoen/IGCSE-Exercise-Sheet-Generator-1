## Photons: the particle nature of light

Electromagnetic radiation has a **particulate nature** as well as a wave nature. The "particles" of EM radiation are called **photons** — discrete packets ("quanta") of EM energy that travel at the speed of light.

### Energy of a photon

A photon of frequency $f$ has energy

$$
E = h f,
$$

where $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J s}$ is the **Planck constant**. Using $c = f\lambda$ for EM radiation in vacuum:

$$
E = \frac{h c}{\lambda}.
$$

Higher-frequency (shorter-wavelength) photons carry more energy: a single γ-ray photon carries far more energy than a single radio photon.

### The electronvolt

The **electronvolt** (eV) is a convenient unit of energy on the atomic scale:

$$
1\ \text{eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}.
$$

It is the kinetic energy gained by an electron accelerated through a potential difference of 1 V. Useful conversions:

- a visible-light photon ($\lambda \approx 500\ \text{nm}$) has energy $\approx 2.5\ \text{eV}$,
- an X-ray photon ($\lambda \approx 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$) has energy $\sim 10^{4}\ \text{eV}$,
- an electron accelerated through $100\ \text{V}$ gains $100\ \text{eV}$ of kinetic energy.

To convert between eV and J: multiply by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ (eV → J) or divide (J → eV).

### Momentum of a photon

A photon also carries **momentum**:

$$
p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h f}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}.
$$

Although photons have zero rest mass, they have a non-zero momentum equal to $E/c$. The radiation-pressure idea (photons pushing on a surface they strike) follows from this.

## Photoelectric effect

When EM radiation of high enough frequency strikes a metal surface, **electrons are emitted**. The emitted electrons are called **photoelectrons**, and the phenomenon is the **photoelectric effect**.

### Threshold frequency and work function

Each metal has a minimum photon frequency, the **threshold frequency** $f_{0}$, below which **no** electrons are emitted, however bright the light. Equivalently, there is a maximum wavelength — the **threshold wavelength** $\lambda_{0}$ — above which no emission occurs:

$$
f_{0} = \frac{c}{\lambda_{0}}.
$$

The **work function** $\Phi$ is the **minimum energy** needed to remove an electron from the surface of the metal:

$$
\Phi = h f_{0}.
$$

Different metals have different work functions (typically $2$–$5\ \text{eV}$ for common metals). Caesium has a small work function; tungsten and platinum have larger ones.

### Einstein's photoelectric equation

A single photon delivers all its energy to a single electron in a one-to-one interaction. If the photon energy $hf$ exceeds the work function $\Phi$, the electron escapes with kinetic energy up to a maximum:

$$
h f = \Phi + \tfrac{1}{2} m v_{\text{max}}^{2}.
$$

This rearranges to

$$
\tfrac{1}{2} m v_{\text{max}}^{2} = h f - \Phi = h(f - f_{0}).
$$

So the maximum KE of photoelectrons depends **linearly on frequency** — not on intensity.

### Why classical wave theory fails

A wave model predicts that intensity (rate of energy delivery) should determine the kinetic energy of emitted electrons, and that emission should occur at any frequency given enough time. Observations contradict this:

- **No emission below the threshold frequency**, however intense the light.
- **Immediate emission** at or above the threshold, even at very low intensity.
- **Maximum KE depends on frequency**, not on intensity.
- **Number of photoelectrons (i.e. photoelectric current) depends on intensity**, not on the kinetic energy of individual emissions.

These observations are explained by the **photon model**: light arrives in discrete photons each carrying $hf$. A single photon-electron interaction either has enough energy to free an electron ($hf \geq \Phi$) or it doesn't.

### Why max KE is independent of intensity but current is proportional to it

A more intense beam of the **same frequency** contains **more photons per second**, but each photon still has energy $hf$. So:

- The **maximum kinetic energy** of any individual emitted electron is $hf - \Phi$, regardless of how many photons arrive — it depends only on $f$.
- The **rate of emission** of photoelectrons (photoelectric current) is proportional to the rate of arriving photons, which is proportional to the intensity.

So doubling the intensity doubles the current but does not change the maximum KE.

## Wave-particle duality

The photoelectric effect (and Compton scattering — not required here) provides strong evidence for the **particulate** nature of EM radiation.

But classical wave phenomena — **interference** (Young's double slit, diffraction grating) and **diffraction** — also occur with light, providing evidence for the **wave** nature.

So light has **both** wave and particle aspects: depending on the experiment, one face shows up more strongly than the other. This is **wave–particle duality**.

### De Broglie hypothesis

If light (a wave phenomenon) can act like particles, perhaps particles (electrons, atoms) can act like waves. De Broglie proposed that any moving particle has an associated **wavelength**:

$$
\lambda = \frac{h}{p},
$$

where $p = mv$ is the momentum. This is the **de Broglie wavelength**.

Worked example: an electron at $v = 4.9 \times 10^{7}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$ has momentum $m_{e} v = (9.11 \times 10^{-31})(4.9 \times 10^{7}) = 4.46 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{kg m s}^{-1}$. So $\lambda = (6.63 \times 10^{-34})/(4.46 \times 10^{-23}) = 1.49 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{m} \approx 0.015\ \text{nm}$ — comparable to atomic spacings.

### Electron diffraction

When electrons (or other particles) are fired at a crystal lattice (e.g. through thin graphite), they produce a **diffraction pattern** of bright concentric rings on a fluorescent screen behind. The ring pattern is exactly what would be expected of waves with wavelength $\lambda = h/p$ being diffracted by the regular lattice spacing.

This is direct experimental evidence for the **wave nature of particles**: only waves diffract, yet electrons do — so electrons must exhibit a wave aspect.

A faster electron has more momentum, so a **shorter de Broglie wavelength** ($\lambda = h/p$). Shorter $\lambda$ diffracts less strongly, so the diffraction rings appear **closer together** (smaller diffraction angles). Slowing the electrons (e.g. by reducing the accelerating p.d.) increases $\lambda$ and spreads the rings further apart.

Use the de Broglie equation:

- Find $p$ from kinetic energy: for non-relativistic electrons, $E_{\text{K}} = p^{2}/(2m)$, so $p = \sqrt{2 m E_{\text{K}}}$.
- For an electron accelerated from rest through a p.d. $V$, $E_{\text{K}} = e V$, and $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2m_{e} e V}$.

## Energy levels in atoms

In an isolated atom (e.g. atomic hydrogen), electrons can only occupy certain **discrete energy levels** — they cannot have any energy in between. The lowest level is the **ground state**, and the others are **excited states**.

By convention, energies are usually written as **negative** numbers, with $E = 0$ corresponding to the electron just free of the atom (ionised). For hydrogen, the ground state is at $E_{1} = -13.6\ \text{eV}$; higher excited states approach zero.

### Emission spectrum

When an excited electron drops from a higher level $E_{2}$ to a lower level $E_{1}$ (with $E_{1} < E_{2}$, both negative), it emits a single photon of energy

$$
h f = E_{2} - E_{1}.
$$

Note: both energies are negative; their difference (a positive number) is the photon energy.

Because the levels are discrete, only certain photon energies — and hence certain frequencies / wavelengths — are emitted. The resulting **emission spectrum** consists of a series of **sharp, discrete lines** on a dark background, with each line at a specific wavelength corresponding to one possible transition. The pattern of lines is a "fingerprint" of the element.

### Absorption spectrum

When white light passes through a cool gas, photons whose energies exactly match a possible upward transition are absorbed (electrons are bumped from a lower level to a higher one). The transmitted light, when dispersed, shows **dark lines on a bright continuous background** — the **absorption spectrum** of the gas.

The dark absorption lines occur at the **same wavelengths** as the emission lines of the same gas — both probe the same set of energy-level differences.

### Calculations

For a transition between two known levels:

$$
hf = E_{2} - E_{1}, \qquad \lambda = \frac{hc}{E_{2} - E_{1}}.
$$

Work in **consistent units** — convert energies to joules (multiply eV by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$) before computing wavelength in metres, or carry through the eV–nm conversion $hc \approx 1240\ \text{eV nm}$ for a quick estimate.
