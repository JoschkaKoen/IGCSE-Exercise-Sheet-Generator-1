## Gravitational fields

### Definition

A **gravitational field** is a region of space in which a mass experiences a force due to other masses. The **gravitational field strength** $g$ at a point is the **gravitational force per unit mass** acting on a small test mass placed there:

$$
g = \frac{F}{m}.
$$

Unit: $\text{N kg}^{-1}$ (equivalently $\text{m s}^{-2}$ — the acceleration of free fall in the field, in the absence of other forces).

$g$ is a **vector** with direction pointing the way the force on a positive test mass would act — towards the source mass.

### Field lines

A gravitational field is represented by **field lines** that point in the direction of the force on a positive test mass:

- around a **point mass** or a **uniform sphere** (treated as a point mass from outside), the field lines are radial, pointing **inwards** towards the centre.
- near the **surface of the Earth** over a small region, the field lines are approximately **parallel and equally spaced**, pointing vertically downwards — a **uniform field**.

The density of field lines indicates the strength: closer lines → stronger field.

## Newton's law of gravitation

For two **point masses** $m_{1}, m_{2}$ separated by distance $r$, the force on each is

$$
F = \frac{G m_{1} m_{2}}{r^{2}},
$$

attractive along the line joining them. The constant $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N m}^{2}\ \text{kg}^{-2}$ is the **universal gravitational constant**.

### Spheres treated as point masses

For a **uniform sphere** (or a spherically symmetric body like a planet or star), the gravitational field at any point **outside** the sphere is identical to that of a point mass equal to the total mass located at the sphere's centre. So if you are above the Earth's surface, you can treat the Earth as a point mass at its centre.

For points **inside** a uniform sphere the result is different, but this is not required by the syllabus.

### Field strength due to a point mass

Substitute the gravitational force on a small test mass $m$ at distance $r$ from a point mass $M$:

$$
F = \frac{G M m}{r^{2}}, \qquad g = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{G M}{r^{2}}.
$$

So $g$ at distance $r$ from a point mass (or outside a uniform sphere of mass $M$):

$$
g = \frac{G M}{r^{2}}.
$$

$g$ is in $\text{N kg}^{-1}$; it falls off as $1/r^{2}$ as you move further from the source.

### Why $g$ is approximately constant near the Earth's surface

The Earth has radius $R \approx 6.4 \times 10^{6}\ \text{m}$. If you rise from the surface to height $h$, the distance from the centre changes from $R$ to $R + h$. For $h \ll R$ (any reasonable building or mountain), $(R + h)/R \approx 1$, so $g$ changes only fractionally. For example, doubling your height above ground from $5\ \text{m}$ to $10\ \text{m}$ changes $r$ by only $5\ \text{m}$ out of $6.4 \times 10^{6}\ \text{m}$ — about one part in a million. Within the laboratory, $g$ is effectively constant.

## Orbital motion in a gravitational field

For a satellite of mass $m$ in a **circular orbit** of radius $r$ around a central body of mass $M$, the gravitational force provides the centripetal force:

$$
\frac{G M m}{r^{2}} = \frac{m v^{2}}{r}.
$$

Cancel $m$ (orbital speed does not depend on the satellite's mass) and solve:

$$
v = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}}.
$$

The period $T$ follows from $T = 2\pi r / v$:

$$
T = 2\pi r \cdot \sqrt{\frac{r}{G M}} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{r^{3}}{G M}}, \quad\text{so}\quad T^{2} = \frac{4\pi^{2}}{G M} \cdot r^{3}.
$$

This is **Kepler's third law** for circular orbits: $T^{2} \propto r^{3}$. A plot of $T^{2}$ against $r^{3}$ is a straight line through the origin with gradient $4\pi^{2}/(GM)$, allowing the central mass to be deduced from orbital data.

### Geostationary orbit

A **geostationary** satellite:

- remains directly above the same point on the Earth's surface (so a fixed dish on the ground always points to it),
- has an orbital **period of 24 hours** (equal to the Earth's rotation period),
- orbits **from west to east** (same sense as the Earth's rotation),
- must be in an orbit **directly above the equator**.

For a satellite to appear fixed in the sky relative to the rotating Earth, it must orbit with the same angular speed as the Earth's rotation, in the same direction, and in the equatorial plane (otherwise it would drift north–south during the day). The required radius is found from $T = 24\ \text{h}$ and $T^{2} = 4\pi^{2} r^{3}/(GM_{\text{Earth}})$, giving $r \approx 4.2 \times 10^{7}\ \text{m}$ (i.e. about $3.6 \times 10^{7}\ \text{m}$ above the Earth's surface).

## Gravitational potential

**Gravitational potential** $\phi$ at a point is the **work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to that point**.

$$
\phi = \frac{W}{m}.
$$

Unit: $\text{J kg}^{-1}$.

The potential is defined to be **zero at infinity**. As the test mass falls towards the source mass, the gravitational force does positive work on it, so $W < 0$ (the work done against the gravitational force when moving from infinity to the point is negative — you do not have to do any work, gravity does it). Hence $\phi$ is **negative** everywhere except at infinity.

For a point mass $M$ at distance $r$:

$$
\phi = -\frac{G M}{r}.
$$

$\phi$ is a **scalar**. For several source masses, total potential is the algebraic sum of the individual potentials.

### Gravitational potential energy of two point masses

If a test mass $m$ is placed at a point where the potential is $\phi$, the **gravitational potential energy** of the pair is

$$
E_{\text{P}} = m \phi = -\frac{G M m}{r}.
$$

Like potential, $E_{\text{P}}$ is **negative** and approaches zero only at infinite separation. The closer the masses, the more negative the potential energy (more deeply bound).

### Connection with $\Delta E_{\text{P}} = mg\Delta h$

For small changes of height near the Earth's surface, $r$ changes only slightly and the change in potential energy can be approximated by $mg\Delta h$. For larger changes (a satellite moving to a higher orbit) you must use $-GMm/r$ at the two radii and take the difference.

Example. A satellite of mass $m$ moves from radius $r_{1}$ to radius $r_{2}$ (with $r_{2} > r_{1}$):

$$
\Delta E_{\text{P}} = -\frac{G M m}{r_{2}} - \left(-\frac{G M m}{r_{1}}\right) = GMm\left(\frac{1}{r_{1}} - \frac{1}{r_{2}}\right).
$$

This is positive (energy supplied to the satellite to raise it).

### Escape velocity (using energy conservation)

For an object to escape from radius $r$ to infinity, its kinetic energy must equal the magnitude of its gravitational potential energy:

$$
\tfrac{1}{2} m v_{\text{esc}}^{2} = \frac{G M m}{r}, \qquad v_{\text{esc}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 G M}{r}}.
$$

For the Earth's surface, $v_{\text{esc}} \approx 11\ \text{km s}^{-1}$. The escape speed does not depend on the launched mass.
