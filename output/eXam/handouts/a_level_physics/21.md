## Alternating current basics

An **alternating current** (a.c.) periodically reverses direction. Mains supply is sinusoidal a.c.: $I$ or $V$ varies as a sine wave of time.

A sinusoidal alternating current has the form

$$
I = I_{0} \sin (\omega t), \qquad V = V_{0} \sin (\omega t).
$$

(The voltage and current may differ in phase, but for a purely resistive load they are in phase, which is the case in this syllabus.)

### Key terms

- **Period** $T$ — time for one complete cycle (one full sine wave). Unit: s.
- **Frequency** $f$ — number of cycles per second. $f = 1/T$. Unit: Hz. Mains in many countries is $50\ \text{Hz}$ or $60\ \text{Hz}$.
- **Angular frequency** $\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi/T$. Unit: $\text{rad s}^{-1}$.
- **Peak value** $I_{0}$ or $V_{0}$ — the maximum value attained during a cycle. Sometimes called amplitude.
- **Peak-to-peak value** $2 I_{0}$ — the difference between the maximum and minimum (i.e. $+I_{0}$ to $-I_{0}$). Used when measuring on an oscilloscope.

### Reading a CRO trace

Same as for any wave (Topic 7):

- horizontal divisions × time-base setting → period $T$, so $f = 1/T$.
- vertical divisions × $y$-gain setting → peak voltage $V_{0}$ (measure from the centre line to the peak; or peak-to-peak then divide by 2).

## Power delivered by an a.c. current to a resistor

For a resistive load $R$, the instantaneous power dissipated when the current is $I(t)$ is

$$
P(t) = I(t)^{2} R.
$$

With $I = I_{0}\sin(\omega t)$:

$$
P(t) = I_{0}^{2} R \sin^{2}(\omega t).
$$

The graph of $P(t)$ is a sinusoid squared — always non-negative, with peak $P_{0} = I_{0}^{2} R$ and minimum zero, oscillating at twice the frequency of $I$.

The **mean** of $\sin^{2}(\omega t)$ over a complete cycle is $\tfrac{1}{2}$. So the **average power** dissipated is

$$
\langle P \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} I_{0}^{2} R = \tfrac{1}{2} P_{\text{peak}}.
$$

Average a.c. power in a resistor is **half the peak power**.

## Root-mean-square (r.m.s.) values

The r.m.s. current $I_{\text{r.m.s.}}$ is defined as the **steady direct current** that would dissipate the same average power in the same resistor. From $\langle P \rangle = I_{\text{r.m.s.}}^{2} R$ and $\langle P \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} I_{0}^{2} R$:

$$
I_{\text{r.m.s.}}^{2} = \tfrac{1}{2} I_{0}^{2}, \qquad I_{\text{r.m.s.}} = \frac{I_{0}}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$

Similarly:

$$
V_{\text{r.m.s.}} = \frac{V_{0}}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$

(For non-sinusoidal waveforms the $\sqrt{2}$ factor is different, but the syllabus only requires the sinusoidal case.)

The reason for the $\sqrt{2}$: r.m.s. is the **root mean square**, i.e. $\sqrt{\langle I^{2} \rangle}$, and $\langle \sin^{2} \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

### Why r.m.s. matters

Quoted a.c. voltages and currents are conventionally **r.m.s. values**. "$230\ \text{V}$ mains" means $V_{\text{r.m.s.}} = 230\ \text{V}$, with peak $V_{0} = 230\sqrt{2} \approx 325\ \text{V}$ and peak-to-peak $\approx 650\ \text{V}$. Components must be rated for the peak voltage, not the r.m.s.

Average power on a resistor in terms of r.m.s.:

$$
\langle P \rangle = I_{\text{r.m.s.}}^{2} R = V_{\text{r.m.s.}}^{2} / R = V_{\text{r.m.s.}} I_{\text{r.m.s.}}.
$$

These take the same form as the d.c. expressions — that's the whole point of defining r.m.s.

## Rectification

**Rectification** is the process of converting an alternating voltage (or current) into a one-direction (d.c.-like) voltage. Done using diodes, which conduct in only one direction.

### Half-wave rectification

A **single diode** in series with the load passes only the positive half of each input cycle; during the negative half, the diode is reverse-biased and no current flows.

Output graph: positive half-sinusoids alternating with flat (zero) gaps. Period of output equals period of input. The mean output voltage over a cycle is $V_{0}/\pi \approx 0.32 V_{0}$, which is much less than the peak.

Disadvantages: half of the input power is wasted (the negative halves are lost); output is very uneven.

### Full-wave rectification (bridge rectifier)

A **bridge rectifier** uses **four diodes** arranged so that the current through the load always flows the same way, regardless of which terminal of the a.c. supply is positive.

For one half-cycle, two diodes conduct (the pair that lets current through from + supply to + load and back from − load to − supply). On the other half-cycle, the other two diodes take over. Either way, the load sees current in the same direction.

Output graph: a continuous series of positive half-sinusoids (every input half-cycle has been flipped to be positive). The output frequency is **twice** the input frequency; no flat gaps. Mean output voltage is $2V_{0}/\pi \approx 0.64 V_{0}$ — double the half-wave case.

Advantages: makes full use of the input, smoother output, easier to filter.

### Drawing the rectifier diagrams

For half-wave: a.c. source in series with a single diode in series with the load $R$. Current can only flow during one polarity.

For full-wave bridge: four diodes arranged as the four arms of a "diamond"; the a.c. input connects to the two opposite vertices of the diamond, the load $R$ connects across the other two vertices. The direction of the diodes is chosen so that for either polarity of the a.c. source, the same pair of opposite-vertex load terminals is "positive" — hence current always flows the same way through $R$.

## Smoothing with a capacitor

The output of a rectifier is still bumpy ("rippled"). To smooth it, place a capacitor $C$ **in parallel with the load** $R$.

### How it works

- During the **rising part** of each output pulse, the capacitor charges up to (close to) the peak voltage. Current flows from the rectifier through the diodes into the capacitor as well as through the load.
- During the **falling part** of each pulse (and during any gap before the next pulse), the diodes are reverse-biased (the capacitor's voltage is higher than the rectifier's instantaneous output). The capacitor then **discharges through the load** $R$, supplying current to it. The voltage across the load falls exponentially with time constant $RC$ (Topic 19).
- At the next rising peak, the capacitor recharges to the peak again. The process repeats.

The output voltage now varies between a maximum (the peak) and some lower value (set by how much the capacitor discharges between peaks). The difference is the **ripple voltage**.

### Factors that affect ripple

- **Larger capacitance $C$** → larger reservoir of charge → less voltage drop between peaks → smaller ripple. Big smoothing capacitors (mF) give a near-d.c. output.
- **Larger load resistance $R$** → smaller load current → capacitor discharges more slowly → smaller ripple.
- **Higher rectified frequency** (full-wave, twice input frequency) → shorter time between peaks for capacitor to discharge → smaller ripple. (One reason full-wave rectification is preferred over half-wave.)

Equivalently, large $RC$ (longer discharge time constant) compared with the time between successive peaks → smoother output.

### Drawing smoothed output

On the same axes as the unsmoothed rectified output, draw the actual capacitor voltage: it rises along the rising part of each peak (when the rectifier supplies it) and falls exponentially during the gap until the next rising peak. The result is a wave that hovers near the peak, with small dips between peaks. The deeper the dips, the more ripple; the smoother the curve, the better the d.c. approximation.

### Purpose of the capacitor in summary

The smoothing capacitor reduces the **ripple** in the output of a rectifier, producing a steadier d.c. voltage suitable for sensitive electronics.
