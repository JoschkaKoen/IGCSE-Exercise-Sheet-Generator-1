## Work, energy and power

### Work done by a force

**Work** is done when a force moves its point of application along the line of the force. The work done by a constant force $F$ producing a displacement $s$ is

$$
W = F \cdot s \cdot \cos\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the force and the displacement. Only the **component of the force in the direction of the displacement** does work.

Unit: $\text{J} = \text{N m}$. Work is a scalar.

Special cases:

- force in the same direction as motion ($\theta = 0$): $W = Fs$, positive work, energy transferred **to** the object.
- force perpendicular to motion ($\theta = 90°$): $W = 0$. The normal contact force on a car driving on a level road does no work.
- force opposite to motion ($\theta = 180°$): $W = -Fs$, negative work, energy transferred **from** the object (e.g. friction).

For an object pushed along a slope at angle $\alpha$ from horizontal, the work done against gravity in rising a vertical height $h$ is $mgh$, while the work done by a horizontal applied force over the slope length $L$ involves $\cos\alpha$.

### Principle of conservation of energy

Energy is **never created or destroyed** — only transferred from one form to another, or from one object to another. In a closed system, the total energy is constant.

When you set up an energy equation, list every form energy starts as and ends as. Common forms in this syllabus: kinetic, gravitational potential, elastic potential, electrical, thermal, sound, chemical.

A ball rolling down a frictionless ramp converts gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy: $mgh = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}$, so $v = \sqrt{2gh}$. With friction, some of the GPE becomes thermal energy of the ramp and the surroundings.

### Efficiency

The **efficiency** of a system is

$$
\text{efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{total energy input}} \times 100\%.
$$

Equivalently, using powers:

$$
\text{efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful power output}}{\text{total power input}} \times 100\%.
$$

Efficiency is always less than 100% in a real system because some input energy becomes "useless" forms — usually thermal energy.

For an electric motor driving a hoist with efficiency $\eta$ at voltage $V$ drawing current $I$, the useful mechanical output power is $\eta V I$. From this you can find a load force, a lift speed, or a tension.

## Power

**Power** is the rate of doing work, or equivalently the rate of transferring energy:

$$
P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}.
$$

Unit: $\text{W} = \text{J s}^{-1}$. Power is a scalar.

### Power, force and velocity

For an object moving at velocity $v$ under a force $F$ acting along the direction of motion, in a short time $\Delta t$ the displacement is $v\,\Delta t$ and the work done is $F v\,\Delta t$. Dividing by $\Delta t$:

$$
P = F v.
$$

This is one of the most useful results in mechanics.

- For a car cruising at constant velocity $v$ on a level road, the engine must supply just enough power to balance the total resistive force: $P = F_{\text{resist}} \cdot v$. Doubling the speed roughly **quadruples** the resistive power if drag scales with $v^{2}$.
- For lifting a weight $mg$ vertically at constant speed $v$, the useful output power is $P = mg \cdot v$.
- For an aircraft or hovercraft hovering at a fixed height, the lift force equals the weight, and the power needed is large because the air must be accelerated downwards continuously.

## Gravitational potential energy

In a **uniform** gravitational field (close to a planet's surface), the change in gravitational potential energy of mass $m$ rising or falling through a vertical height $\Delta h$ is

$$
\Delta E_{\text{P}} = m g \Delta h.
$$

### Derivation

The work done **against** gravity in raising a mass $m$ slowly (no change in kinetic energy) through height $\Delta h$ equals the energy gained as gravitational potential energy:

- the gravitational force on the mass is $mg$ downwards,
- the applied force needed to lift it slowly is $mg$ upwards,
- the work done by this applied force is $W = F \cdot s = mg \cdot \Delta h$,
- this work becomes $\Delta E_{\text{P}}$.

So $\Delta E_{\text{P}} = mg \Delta h$.

To use $\Delta E_{\text{P}} = mg\Delta h$ you need to know $m$ and $\Delta h$ (and $g$). You do not need to know speed or time.

## Kinetic energy

The kinetic energy of an object of mass $m$ moving at speed $v$ is

$$
E_{\text{K}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}.
$$

### Derivation

Apply a resultant force $F$ to a mass $m$ initially at rest. The mass accelerates uniformly from $0$ to $v$ over a displacement $s$. From $v^{2} = u^{2} + 2as$ with $u = 0$,

$$
s = \frac{v^{2}}{2a}.
$$

The work done on the mass by the resultant force is

$$
W = F \cdot s = m a \cdot \frac{v^{2}}{2a} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}.
$$

All of this work becomes kinetic energy (no other forces, no other energy transfers), so $E_{\text{K}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}$.

### Kinetic energy and momentum

Combining $p = mv$ and $E_{\text{K}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}$:

$$
E_{\text{K}} = \frac{p^{2}}{2m}.
$$

This is handy when momentum is given but not velocity. For a momentum change from $p_{1}$ to $p_{2}$ at constant mass, the change in kinetic energy is $(p_{2}^{2} - p_{1}^{2}) / (2m)$.

## Applying energy methods

A useful strategy for problems that mix mechanics and energy:

1. **Identify the initial and final states.** Write down kinetic and potential energies in each.
2. **List any work done by external forces** (friction, applied push). Friction usually takes energy out; an applied force can add it.
3. **Conservation of energy**: $E_{\text{initial}} + W_{\text{external in}} = E_{\text{final}} + W_{\text{lost as heat etc.}}$.

Examples:

- A box pushed at **constant velocity** up a ramp of length $L$ rising by $h$: $E_{\text{K}}$ doesn't change, so all the work done by the push goes into $\Delta E_{\text{P}}$ + work done against friction.
- A block sliding into a spring with kinetic energy $E_{\text{K}}$ on a frictionless surface: at maximum compression $x$, all kinetic energy has become elastic potential energy $\tfrac{1}{2} k x^{2}$.
- A ball released from height $h_{1}$ that bounces to height $h_{2}$ : the ratio $h_{2}/h_{1}$ is the fraction of mechanical energy retained, $h_{2}/h_{1} = (v_{\text{up}}/v_{\text{down}})^{2}$.
- A projectile thrown at different launch angles to the same height: ignore the angle — energy conservation gives the same final speed (only the height matters), and direction can be found from components.
