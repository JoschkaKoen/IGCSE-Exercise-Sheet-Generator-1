## Principle of superposition

When two or more waves overlap at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is the **vector sum** of the displacements that each wave would produce on its own.

The waves pass through each other and emerge unchanged on the other side. Superposition is the foundation for everything in this topic.

If two waves of amplitudes $A_{1}$ and $A_{2}$ meet:

- in phase (crest meets crest): resultant amplitude $= A_{1} + A_{2}$ (constructive interference).
- exactly out of phase (crest meets trough, phase difference $\pi$): resultant amplitude $= |A_{1} - A_{2}|$ (destructive interference).
- general phase difference $\phi$: resultant amplitude lies between these extremes.

Note that for **intensity**, $I \propto A^{2}$. Two equal-amplitude waves meeting in phase give an intensity of $(2A)^{2} = 4A^{2}$, i.e. **four times** the intensity of one wave alone.

## Stationary (standing) waves

When two identical progressive waves travel in **opposite directions** and overlap, they form a **stationary wave**. Common examples: a wave on a string reflected from a fixed end overlaps with the incoming wave; sound waves in an air column reflected from the closed end; microwaves between an emitter and a metal reflector.

### Nodes and antinodes

In a stationary wave:

- **Node** — a point of permanently zero displacement (the two waves always cancel). Distance between adjacent nodes is $\lambda/2$.
- **Antinode** — a point of maximum amplitude (the two waves always reinforce). Distance between adjacent antinodes is $\lambda/2$.
- A node and the next antinode are separated by $\lambda/4$.

Particles between adjacent nodes oscillate **in phase** with each other, but with different amplitudes (largest at the antinode, zero at the nodes). Particles on opposite sides of a node oscillate in **antiphase** (phase difference $\pi$).

Differences from a progressive wave: a stationary wave does not transfer energy along its length, has no continuous progression of the waveform, and has fixed nodes; a progressive wave has the same amplitude everywhere and transfers energy.

### Measuring wavelength from node spacing

Drive a string with a vibrator at frequency $f$ until a stationary pattern forms. Measure the distance between two well-separated nodes and divide by the number of half-wavelengths between them. Then $\lambda$ is known and $v = f\lambda$ gives the wave speed.

For a tube closed at one end and open at the other (resonance tube), the closed end is a displacement **node** and the open end is a displacement **antinode**. The fundamental has $L = \lambda/4$; next resonance at $L = 3\lambda/4$; etc.

For a tube open at both ends, both ends are antinodes; fundamental at $L = \lambda/2$.

A microwave demonstration uses a horn emitter facing a metal sheet; moving a detector between them locates nodes and antinodes. The detector reads minimum signal at nodes.

## Diffraction

**Diffraction** is the spreading of a wave after it passes through a gap or around an obstacle. All waves diffract — water, sound, light, microwaves.

The amount of diffraction depends on the ratio of **wavelength to gap width**:

- gap **much wider** than $\lambda$: very little spreading; the wave passes through nearly undeflected.
- gap **of order** $\lambda$: significant spreading; the wave fans out from the gap.
- gap **smaller than** $\lambda$: extreme spreading; the gap behaves as a near-point source of circular wavefronts.

Demonstrate with water waves in a ripple tank: plane waves approach a barrier with an adjustable gap; you can see the wavefronts curve more strongly as the gap is narrowed. The same principle is why you can hear a person around a corner (the wavelength of speech is around 1 m, comparable to the corner gap) but cannot see them (visible light has $\lambda \sim 500\ \text{nm}$, much smaller than the gap).

## Interference

**Interference** is the superposition of two coherent waves to produce a stable pattern of regions of high amplitude (constructive interference) and low amplitude (destructive interference).

### Coherence

Two sources are **coherent** when they emit waves with a **constant phase difference** (in practice, this also requires the same frequency). Two separate lamps are not coherent — their phase relationship varies randomly, so any interference pattern fluctuates faster than the eye can see and you observe just an average.

To create coherent light from a single source, pass it through two slits in Young's double-slit setup. Both slits get illuminated by the same wavefront, so the two emerging beams have a fixed phase relationship.

### Conditions for a clear interference pattern

To see two-source interference fringes you need:

1. Two sources that are **coherent** (constant phase difference).
2. The two sources of approximately equal **amplitude** (otherwise minima are not very dark).
3. The waves overlap in the region of observation.
4. For transverse waves like light, the two waves must have the **same plane of polarisation**.

### Path difference

For two coherent sources, the condition at a point depends on the **path difference** $\Delta x$ between the two waves arriving there:

- constructive interference: $\Delta x = n\lambda$ (for integer $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$).
- destructive interference: $\Delta x = (n + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$.

### Double-slit (Young's) experiment

For two slits separated by $a$, with a screen at distance $D$ from the slits (assume $D \gg a$), light of wavelength $\lambda$ produces fringes on the screen with a **fringe spacing** $x$ given by

$$
\lambda = \frac{a x}{D}, \qquad x = \frac{\lambda D}{a}.
$$

Bright fringes (maxima) occur where the path difference is an integer multiple of $\lambda$; dark fringes (minima) where it is $(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$. Fringes are equally spaced.

To reduce fringe spacing: increase $a$ (slits further apart), reduce $D$ (screen closer), or use shorter $\lambda$ (more blue light). Note that $x$ is measured between adjacent bright (or adjacent dark) fringes.

## Diffraction grating

A **diffraction grating** has many equally spaced slits — typically hundreds or thousands per millimetre. Each slit acts as a coherent source. The condition for a maximum at angle $\theta$ from the normal to the grating is

$$
d \sin\theta = n \lambda,
$$

where $d$ is the **slit spacing** (centre-to-centre), $n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots$ is the **order**, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength.

Compared with the double slit, a grating gives much **sharper** maxima because more slits contribute — each off-maximum direction is killed by destructive interference from many slits.

### Slit spacing from "lines per mm"

If a grating has $N$ lines per millimetre, then $d = 1/N$ millimetres $= 10^{-3}/N$ metres. For $450$ lines per mm, $d = 1/450\ \text{mm} \approx 2.22\ \text{µm}$.

### Maximum order

For a given grating and wavelength, $\sin\theta = n\lambda/d$ cannot exceed $1$, so the highest visible order is

$$
n_{\text{max}} = \left\lfloor \frac{d}{\lambda} \right\rfloor.
$$

If $d/\lambda = 3.27$, then orders up to $n = 3$ exist; $n = 4$ would require $\sin\theta > 1$ and is not observed.

### Using a grating to determine $\lambda$

Shine collimated light of unknown wavelength normally onto the grating. Measure the angle $\theta_{1}$ of the first-order maximum from the central maximum. Then $\lambda = d \sin\theta_{1}$. Repeating for higher orders and averaging reduces error. The structure and use of a spectrometer beyond this is not required.
