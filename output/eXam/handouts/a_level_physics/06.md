## Forces causing deformation

When a force is applied to a solid object along its length, the object deforms. Two cases (in this syllabus, treated as one-dimensional):

- a **tensile** force stretches the object — produces an **extension** $x$,
- a **compressive** force squeezes the object — produces a **compression**, treated as a negative extension.

The applied force is called the **load**. The change in length from the natural length is the **extension** (or compression).

## Hooke's law and the spring constant

For many materials at small extensions, **the extension is proportional to the load** — this is **Hooke's law**. The constant of proportionality is the **spring constant** $k$:

$$
F = kx \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad k = \frac{F}{x}.
$$

Unit of $k$: $\text{N m}^{-1}$.

Reading a graph:

- A force–extension ($F$ vs $x$) graph has gradient $k$ in the Hookean region.
- An extension–force ($x$ vs $F$) graph has gradient $1/k$ in the Hookean region.

A common trap: if a question plots **length** $L$ against force, the spring constant is still found from the gradient (since $L = L_{0} + F/k$, gradient is $1/k$ — read off carefully).

### Limit of proportionality

Hooke's law holds only up to the **limit of proportionality** — beyond this point the $F$ vs $x$ line curves and the relation is no longer linear. The material may still be elastic (returning to its original length on unloading) for a little further, then becomes plastic.

### Springs in series and parallel

You may need to combine spring constants:

- **Series** (one spring's bottom hangs from another): the same load passes through both, total extension is the sum, so $\dfrac{1}{k_{\text{total}}} = \dfrac{1}{k_{1}} + \dfrac{1}{k_{2}}$.
- **Parallel** (two springs side by side supporting the same load): each takes half the load (if identical), extensions are equal, so $k_{\text{total}} = k_{1} + k_{2}$.

## Stress, strain and the Young modulus

For a wire of uniform cross-section under a tensile load:

- **Stress** $\sigma = \dfrac{F}{A}$, where $F$ is the load and $A$ is the cross-sectional area. Unit: $\text{Pa}$.
- **Strain** $\varepsilon = \dfrac{x}{L_{0}}$, where $x$ is the extension and $L_{0}$ is the original length. Strain has no unit (a ratio of lengths).

The **Young modulus** is the ratio of stress to strain in the Hookean region:

$$
E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{F / A}{x / L_{0}} = \frac{F L_{0}}{A x}.
$$

Unit: $\text{Pa}$ (typically $\sim 10^{11}$ for metals; e.g. steel $\approx 2.0 \times 10^{11}$ Pa).

Young modulus is a property of the **material** — it does not depend on the wire's shape or size. The spring constant $k$, by contrast, depends on both the material and the geometry: $k = EA/L_{0}$.

### Experiment to determine the Young modulus of a metal wire

A standard setup:

1. Clamp one end of a long, thin wire to a fixed support; pass the wire over a pulley at the edge of the bench so the wire hangs vertically below the pulley.
2. Measure the **original length** $L_{0}$ between the clamp and a marker on the wire near the pulley, using a metre rule.
3. Measure the **diameter** $d$ of the wire at several points along the wire using a micrometer screw gauge; average. Calculate $A = \pi d^{2}/4$.
4. Hang weights one at a time, recording the load $F$ and the extension $x$ measured from the marker's displacement against a fixed scale.
5. Plot $F$ against $x$. In the linear region, gradient $= EA/L_{0}$, so $E = \text{gradient} \times L_{0}/A$.

Why a **long, thin wire**? — to make the extension large enough to measure with reasonable precision. Why repeat readings, and why measure $d$ at several points? — to reduce random error and check that the wire is uniform.

## Elastic and plastic behaviour

The behaviour as load increases:

1. **Elastic, linear (Hooke obeyed)** — up to the limit of proportionality. Removing the load returns the object to its original length.
2. **Elastic, non-linear** — between the limit of proportionality and the **elastic limit**. The extension is no longer linear in load, but on unloading the object still returns to its original length.
3. **Plastic** — beyond the elastic limit. On unloading, the object does **not** return to its original length; a permanent extension remains.

Hooke's law is obeyed only in the linear elastic region.

On a force–extension graph for a material taken into the plastic region and then unloaded, the loading line and the unloading line are not the same. The unloading line is parallel to the initial Hookean line but offset to the right (the residual plastic extension when the load reaches zero). The area enclosed between the loading and unloading lines is the energy dissipated as thermal energy in the material.

## Energy stored in a deformed material

The **work done** in extending a material from $0$ to extension $x$ under a load that grows from $0$ to $F$ is the **area under the force–extension graph**.

### Hookean material

When Hooke's law holds, the $F$ vs $x$ graph is a straight line through the origin. The area under it from $0$ to $x$ is a triangle:

$$
E_{\text{P}} = \tfrac{1}{2} F x = \tfrac{1}{2} k x^{2}.
$$

This is the **elastic potential energy** stored in a spring or wire deformed within its limit of proportionality. Other equivalent forms:

$$
E_{\text{P}} = \frac{F^{2}}{2k}.
$$

### Non-Hookean material

For a graph that is not a straight line (a stretched rubber band, a spring beyond its limit of proportionality), the area must be found by counting grid squares or by approximating with trapezia. The same principle applies: **area under the force–extension graph equals the work done on the material**.

### Energy comparison

A common multiple-choice scenario: two materials are extended by the same force, or by the same extension. Using $E_{\text{P}} = \tfrac{1}{2} F x$:

- Same $F$, smaller $k$ (more compliant) → larger $x$ → more energy stored.
- Same $x$, larger $k$ (stiffer) → larger $F$ → more energy stored.

When a stretched spring is released onto a mass, the elastic potential energy stored converts into kinetic energy (and into GPE if the mass rises). Set $\tfrac{1}{2} k x^{2}$ equal to $\tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}$ (plus any $mgh$) to find the projectile speed or height.
