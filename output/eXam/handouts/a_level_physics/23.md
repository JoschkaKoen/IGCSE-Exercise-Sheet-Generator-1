## Mass-energy equivalence

Einstein's special theory of relativity gives the famous equivalence:

$$
E = m c^{2},
$$

where $c = 3.00 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$ is the speed of light in vacuum. A mass $m$ corresponds to an energy $E$ — the two are interchangeable.

In nuclear physics, the masses involved are tiny but $c^{2}$ is enormous, so even a small change in mass corresponds to a large energy. For a mass change $\Delta m$:

$$
\Delta E = c^{2} \Delta m.
$$

A mass change of $1\ \text{u}$ ($1.661 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$) corresponds to $\Delta E = (1.661 \times 10^{-27})(3.00 \times 10^{8})^{2} \approx 1.49 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{J} \approx 931\ \text{MeV}$. So $1\ \text{u} \approx 931\ \text{MeV}/c^{2}$ — a handy conversion factor.

## Nuclear reactions

A general nuclear reaction is written like

$$
^{14}_{7}\text{N} + {}^{4}_{2}\text{He} \to {}^{17}_{8}\text{O} + {}^{1}_{1}\text{H},
$$

with conservation of **nucleon number** (top numbers add: $14 + 4 = 17 + 1$) and **charge** (bottom numbers add: $7 + 2 = 8 + 1$).

Use these conservation laws to fill in unknowns in an equation: identify the species, then balance the top and bottom numbers.

## Mass defect and binding energy

The mass of any nucleus is **less than** the sum of the masses of its constituent nucleons (protons and neutrons measured separately). The difference is called the **mass defect** $\Delta m$:

$$
\Delta m = (Z m_{\text{p}} + N m_{\text{n}}) - m_{\text{nucleus}},
$$

where $Z$ protons and $N$ neutrons combine to form the nucleus of mass $m_{\text{nucleus}}$ (sometimes called $m_{\text{rest}}$).

By Einstein's equation, this "missing" mass has been released as energy when the nucleus formed. To **disassemble** the nucleus back into its constituents you would have to put this energy back in. So the energy required to fully separate a nucleus into its individual protons and neutrons is the **binding energy** $B$:

$$
B = \Delta m \cdot c^{2}.
$$

A more strongly bound nucleus has a larger mass defect and a larger binding energy.

The **binding energy per nucleon** is

$$
\frac{B}{A} \quad\text{(usually given in MeV per nucleon)}.
$$

This is a measure of how tightly each nucleon is held — a useful comparison across different nuclides.

### Binding energy per nucleon vs. nucleon number

A graph of $B/A$ vs $A$ (nucleon number) has a characteristic shape:

- For light nuclei ($A < 20$), $B/A$ rises rapidly with $A$ (very low for $^{1}\text{H}$, $^{2}\text{H}$, $^{3}\text{H}$). Spike at $^{4}_{2}\text{He}$ — particularly stable.
- For medium-mass nuclei around $A \sim 56$ (iron group), $B/A$ reaches its **maximum** of about $8.8\ \text{MeV}$. **Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon** — the most stable nucleus.
- For heavy nuclei ($A > 100$), $B/A$ falls slowly with $A$, reaching about $7.5\ \text{MeV}$ for uranium.

The whole curve is roughly **dome-shaped** with a slow rise from light nuclei to ${}^{56}\text{Fe}$ and a slow fall thereafter.

## Nuclear fusion and fission

The shape of the $B/A$ curve implies that energy is **released** when nuclei move **towards** the iron peak — either by combining light nuclei or by splitting heavy ones.

### Nuclear fusion

**Fusion** is the joining of two light nuclei to form a single heavier nucleus. Example:

$$
^{2}_{1}\text{H} + {}^{3}_{1}\text{H} \to {}^{4}_{2}\text{He} + {}^{1}_{0}\text{n} + \text{energy}.
$$

The product ($^{4}$He) has greater binding energy per nucleon than the reactants ($^{2}$H and $^{3}$H — both at the very low end of the curve), so energy is released. Fusion powers stars (proton–proton chain, CNO cycle).

Fusion requires extremely high temperatures (millions of kelvin) so that the colliding nuclei have enough kinetic energy to overcome their **electrostatic repulsion** and get close enough for the strong nuclear force to take over.

### Nuclear fission

**Fission** is the splitting of a heavy nucleus into two (or more) lighter nuclei. Example:

$$
^{235}_{92}\text{U} + {}^{1}_{0}\text{n} \to {}^{141}_{56}\text{Ba} + {}^{92}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3\, {}^{1}_{0}\text{n} + \text{energy}.
$$

Both products ($^{141}$Ba, $^{92}$Kr) have higher binding energy per nucleon than $^{235}$U, so energy is released.

The release of additional neutrons (3 in the above) makes a **chain reaction** possible: each fission neutron can induce a further fission in a sufficient mass of fuel (the critical mass). This is the basis of nuclear power and atomic weapons.

### Calculating the energy released

For any nuclear reaction:

1. Calculate the total mass of the reactants.
2. Calculate the total mass of the products.
3. The mass change $\Delta m = m_{\text{reactants}} - m_{\text{products}}$ (positive when energy is released).
4. Energy released $\Delta E = c^{2} \Delta m$.

If masses are in kg, $\Delta E$ comes out in joules; if in atomic mass units, use $\Delta E\ (\text{MeV}) = \Delta m\ (\text{u}) \times 931$.

## Radioactive decay

### Random and spontaneous

Radioactive decay is

- **spontaneous** — it occurs without external trigger, and the rate is not affected by temperature, pressure, chemical state, or any normal physical condition; and
- **random** — for any given unstable nucleus you cannot predict when it will decay, only the probability that it will decay in a given time interval.

Evidence for randomness: **fluctuations in count rate**. A Geiger counter held next to a radioactive source registers counts at unevenly spaced intervals — never a steady stream. The mean rate over a long time is well-defined, but individual time intervals between counts vary unpredictably.

### Activity and decay constant

For a sample of $N$ undecayed nuclei of a given radionuclide, the **rate of decay** at any instant is

$$
A = \lambda N.
$$

- $A$ is the **activity** — the **number of decays per unit time**. Unit: **becquerel** (Bq) $= \text{s}^{-1}$.
- $\lambda$ is the **decay constant** — the probability per unit time that any individual nucleus decays. Unit: $\text{s}^{-1}$ (or $\text{min}^{-1}$, $\text{hr}^{-1}$, $\text{yr}^{-1}$, etc.).

$\lambda$ is a property of the nuclide; $N$ is how many you have. A larger sample with the same nuclide has proportionally larger activity.

### Exponential decay

Since $\lambda$ is the **fractional decay rate**, the differential equation for the population is

$$
\frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N,
$$

whose solution is

$$
N = N_{0} e^{-\lambda t}.
$$

The number of undecayed nuclei decreases **exponentially** with time. Because $A = \lambda N$, the activity decays with the same time dependence:

$$
A = A_{0} e^{-\lambda t}.
$$

And so does any quantity proportional to $A$, such as the count rate received by a detector at a fixed position:

$$
\text{count rate} = (\text{count rate})_{0} e^{-\lambda t}.
$$

### Why decay is exponential

For each nucleus, $\lambda$ is the probability per unit time of decaying — independent of all others, independent of how old the nucleus is. In any time interval, the same **fraction** of the surviving nuclei decay. A constant fractional rate produces exponential decay. Equivalently: $\Delta N/\Delta t \propto N$, leading to $N \propto e^{-\lambda t}$.

### Half-life

The **half-life** $t_{1/2}$ is the time for the number of undecayed nuclei (or equivalently the activity, or the count rate) to fall to **half its initial value**.

From $N = N_{0} e^{-\lambda t}$ with $N = N_{0}/2$ at $t = t_{1/2}$:

$$
\tfrac{1}{2} = e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}}, \qquad \ln 2 = \lambda t_{1/2}.
$$

So

$$
\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}} \approx \frac{0.693}{t_{1/2}}.
$$

A larger decay constant means a shorter half-life. Half-lives in the syllabus run from milliseconds to billions of years.

After $n$ half-lives, the surviving fraction is $(1/2)^{n}$. After $5$ half-lives, only about $3\%$ remains.

### Finding $\lambda$ from data

Given an initial activity $A_{0}$ and the activity $A$ at time $t$:

$$
\lambda = \frac{1}{t} \ln\frac{A_{0}}{A}.
$$

Then $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda$.

A useful linearisation: take logs of $A = A_{0} e^{-\lambda t}$:

$$
\ln A = \ln A_{0} - \lambda t.
$$

Plotting $\ln A$ against $t$ gives a straight line with **negative gradient** $-\lambda$ and $y$-intercept $\ln A_{0}$. Use this when several data points are available.
