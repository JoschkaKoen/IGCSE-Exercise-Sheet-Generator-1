## Internal energy

The **internal energy** $U$ of a system is the sum of:

- the **random kinetic energies** of its molecules (translational, and for non-monatomic molecules also rotational and vibrational), and
- the **potential energies** associated with the forces between its molecules.

For a real substance — a solid, a liquid, or a real gas — both contributions matter. In the **ideal-gas** model, intermolecular forces are assumed negligible, so the molecular potential energy is zero and the internal energy is purely kinetic.

Two important properties:

1. **$U$ depends only on the state** of the system (its temperature, pressure, volume, amount of substance) — not on the path taken to reach that state. Two samples in the same state have the same internal energy.
2. **$U$ is a sum over molecules**, not the kinetic energy of the system as a whole. A moving train of gas has kinetic energy of bulk motion, but this is separate from $U$ — $U$ is the energy of the *random* molecular motion in the train's rest frame.

### Temperature and internal energy

Increasing the temperature of an object increases the random kinetic energy of its molecules and so increases its internal energy. The connection is direct.

For an **ideal gas**, every molecule has average translational kinetic energy $\tfrac{3}{2} k T$ (Topic 15). Since intermolecular potential energy is zero, the total internal energy is

$$
U = \tfrac{3}{2} N k T = \tfrac{3}{2} n R T.
$$

So **the internal energy of an ideal gas is directly proportional to the thermodynamic temperature**. Doubling $T$ doubles $U$. Tripling $T$ triples $U$. This is **only** true for an ideal gas — for real substances, $U$ depends on temperature in a more complex way.

For a real solid being heated through a phase change (melting or boiling), $U$ rises because the molecular potential energy rises (bonds being broken), even though the temperature stays constant.

## Work done on or by a gas

When a gas changes volume against an external pressure, mechanical work is done. For a gas at constant pressure $p$ undergoing a small volume change $\Delta V$, the magnitude of the work transferred is

$$
W = p \Delta V.
$$

### Sign convention used in the syllabus

This syllabus writes the first law as $\Delta U = q + W$, where:

- **$W$ is the work done ON the gas** (energy transferred to the gas by mechanical compression).
- $q$ is the energy transferred to the gas by heating.

With this convention:

- when the gas is **compressed**, $\Delta V$ is negative (volume decreases), and the work done **on** the gas is positive — $W = +p|\Delta V|$. The system gains energy from the surroundings.
- when the gas **expands**, $\Delta V$ is positive (volume increases), and the work done **on** the gas is negative — $W = -p|\Delta V|$. The system loses energy to the surroundings (the gas is doing positive work on the surroundings).

Be careful which form a question asks for:

- "work done **on** the gas" — use $W = -p\Delta V$ (positive when compressing).
- "work done **by** the gas" — opposite sign, $W_{\text{by}} = +p\Delta V$ (positive when expanding).

A safer pattern: think about whether energy is being **added to** the gas (compression → $W$ on gas is positive) or **removed from** it (expansion → $W$ on gas is negative).

For a process at constant volume ($\Delta V = 0$), no work is done — neither on nor by the gas.

## First law of thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics states that **energy is conserved when heat and work are exchanged between a system and its surroundings**. In symbols:

$$
\Delta U = q + W,
$$

where:

- $\Delta U$ — increase in internal energy of the gas.
- $q$ — energy transferred to the gas **by heating** (positive when heat flows in, negative when heat flows out).
- $W$ — work done **on** the gas (positive when compressed, negative when expanding).

This is conservation of energy applied to a gas (or any closed thermodynamic system).

### Reading the equation

$\Delta U$ is determined entirely by the change of state of the gas (so by the change in temperature, for an ideal gas). The same $\Delta U$ can be achieved by different combinations of $q$ and $W$:

- All heat, no work: $\Delta U = q$ (constant volume heating).
- All work, no heat: $\Delta U = W$ (adiabatic / insulated compression or expansion).
- Heat in, work out: $\Delta U$ is the difference.

### Applying the first law: standard processes

For an **ideal gas**, the internal-energy change is $\Delta U = \tfrac{3}{2} n R \Delta T = \tfrac{3}{2} N k \Delta T$. So $\Delta U$ depends only on $\Delta T$.

| Process | What stays constant | $\Delta U$ | $W$ (on gas) | $q$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Isothermal | $T$ | $0$ | $W$ (whatever value) | $-W$ (so $q + W = 0$) |
| Constant volume | $V$ | $\tfrac{3}{2}n R \Delta T$ | $0$ | $\Delta U$ |
| Constant pressure | $p$ | $\tfrac{3}{2}n R \Delta T$ | $-p \Delta V$ | $\Delta U - W$ |
| Adiabatic | — (no heat) | varies | $W$ | $0$ |

Note: for an **isothermal** process of an ideal gas, $\Delta T = 0$ so $\Delta U = 0$. From the first law, $q = -W$ — any heat that flows in goes out as work, and vice versa.

For a constant-volume heating (e.g. gas in a sealed rigid container heated by a coil), all the heat supplied goes into internal energy: $q = \Delta U$.

For a constant-pressure expansion (e.g. gas pushing a piston against a constant external pressure), $\Delta V > 0$, so the gas does positive work on the surroundings — $W$ on the gas is negative, and the heat supplied must do double duty: raise the internal energy and supply the expansion work.

### Worked example: two-step process

A sample of ideal gas at temperature $T$ and internal energy $U$ undergoes two steps:

1. **Compression** to temperature $3T$. Work $W$ is done on the gas.
2. **Cooling at constant volume** until temperature drops to $2T$.

For each step:

**Step 1** (compression $T \to 3T$):

- $\Delta U_{1} = \tfrac{3}{2}nR(3T - T) = 3nRT$. For an ideal gas $U = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT$, so $U_{\text{initial}} = U$ at temperature $T$ implies $U_{\text{after step 1}} = 3U$, i.e. $\Delta U_{1} = 2U$.
- $W_{1}$ on gas $= +W$ (given).
- $q_{1} = \Delta U_{1} - W_{1} = 2U - W$.

**Step 2** (constant-volume cooling $3T \to 2T$):

- $\Delta U_{2} = \tfrac{3}{2}nR(2T - 3T) = -\tfrac{3}{2}nRT$. Since $U = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT$ at temperature $T$, this is $\Delta U_{2} = -U$.
- $W_{2} = 0$ (constant volume).
- $q_{2} = \Delta U_{2} - W_{2} = -U$. (Negative — heat flows out of the gas.)

Check: total $\Delta U = 2U - U = U$, taking the system from temperature $T$ to $2T$, which corresponds to $U \to 2U$ for the ideal gas — consistent.

### Heat capacity at constant volume

For a constant-volume process with an ideal gas, $q = \Delta U = \tfrac{3}{2} n R \Delta T$. So the molar heat capacity at constant volume is $C_{V} = \tfrac{3}{2} R$ for a monatomic ideal gas. (Not in the syllabus to use the symbol $C_{V}$, but the result $q = \tfrac{3}{2} n R \Delta T$ for constant-volume heating is.)

### Heating without temperature change

If heat is supplied during a phase change at constant pressure (e.g. boiling water), the temperature stays constant but the internal energy still rises (potential energy of separated molecules), and the gas does expansion work. The first law applies: $\Delta U = q + W$, with $q$ the latent heat input and $W$ the (negative, gas-expanding) work term.
