## Progressive waves

A **wave** transfers energy from one place to another without any net transfer of matter. The particles of the medium oscillate about fixed equilibrium positions; only the disturbance (and the energy it carries) propagates. Familiar examples: a transverse wave on a stretched rope, a longitudinal wave on a slinky spring, surface ripples on a water tank, sound in air.

### Key terms

- **Displacement** $y$ — how far a particle has moved from its equilibrium position at a given instant. A vector.
- **Amplitude** $A$ — the maximum displacement from equilibrium.
- **Wavelength** $\lambda$ — the shortest distance along the wave between two points that are oscillating **in phase** (e.g. two adjacent crests).
- **Period** $T$ — the time for one complete oscillation of a particle.
- **Frequency** $f$ — the number of complete oscillations per second; $f = 1/T$. Unit: hertz, $\text{Hz}$.
- **Speed** $v$ — how fast the wave profile (a crest, say) travels along the medium.
- **Phase difference** — the fraction of a cycle by which one oscillation leads or lags another. Usually expressed in radians (a full cycle is $2\pi$) or degrees (a full cycle is $360°$).

Two points one wavelength apart are in phase (phase difference 0 or $2\pi$). Two points half a wavelength apart are exactly out of phase (phase difference $\pi$).

### The wave equation

In one period $T$, the wave moves forward by exactly one wavelength $\lambda$. So speed $= \text{distance} / \text{time} = \lambda / T = \lambda f$:

$$
v = f \lambda.
$$

This derives directly from the definitions of speed, frequency and wavelength, and applies to every kind of progressive wave.

### Reading a CRO trace

A **cathode-ray oscilloscope** plots a voltage signal — for sound, the output of a microphone — against time. Two controls matter:

- **time-base** (s per horizontal division): convert horizontal distance on the screen into time. Read off the period $T$ as the horizontal distance between two adjacent peaks, then $f = 1/T$.
- **$y$-gain** (volts per vertical division): convert vertical distance into voltage. The amplitude in volts is the peak displacement from the centre line.

If a question gives "time-base $5\ \text{ms}/\text{div}$" and the wave shows one full cycle every $4$ divisions, then $T = 4 \times 5\ \text{ms} = 20\ \text{ms}$ and $f = 50\ \text{Hz}$.

### Intensity of a progressive wave

A wave transfers energy. The **intensity** at a point is the power crossing unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation:

$$
I = \frac{P}{A}.
$$

Unit: $\text{W m}^{-2}$.

Intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude:

$$
I \propto A^{2}.
$$

For a point source radiating equally in all directions in three dimensions, the wavefronts are spheres; the surface area at distance $r$ is $4\pi r^{2}$, so

$$
I = \frac{P}{4\pi r^{2}}, \qquad I \propto \frac{1}{r^{2}}.
$$

Doubling the distance reduces the intensity by a factor of four, which corresponds to halving the amplitude (since $I \propto A^{2}$).

## Transverse and longitudinal waves

### Transverse waves

The particles oscillate **perpendicular** to the direction of energy travel. A wave on a rope, all electromagnetic waves, and S-waves in the Earth are transverse.

### Longitudinal waves

The particles oscillate **parallel** to the direction of energy travel. Sound waves in any medium, P-waves in the Earth, and compressions on a slinky are longitudinal. The wave consists of **compressions** (regions of higher pressure / particles bunched together) and **rarefactions** (regions of lower pressure / particles spread out).

### Graphical representations

A graph of **particle displacement vs position** at a single instant looks sinusoidal for both kinds of wave. The difference: for a transverse wave, the displacement axis really is the perpendicular displacement of the medium; for a longitudinal wave, the displacement axis is the small back-and-forth displacement of particles along the direction of motion (positive in one chosen direction, negative the other).

A graph of **particle displacement vs time** at a single point in space is also sinusoidal for both kinds. Period $T$ is read off this graph.

## Doppler effect (moving source, stationary observer)

When the source of a sound wave moves relative to a stationary observer, the observed frequency differs from the source frequency.

- Source approaching the observer: wavefronts ahead of the source are compressed, so wavelength is shortened and observed frequency is **higher** than emitted.
- Source receding from the observer: wavefronts behind the source are spread out, so wavelength is longer and observed frequency is **lower** than emitted.

The formula (for a source moving with speed $v_{\text{s}}$ along the line to the observer; wave speed $v$, emitted frequency $f_{\text{s}}$, observed frequency $f_{\text{o}}$):

$$
f_{\text{o}} = \frac{v \cdot f_{\text{s}}}{v \pm v_{\text{s}}}.
$$

The sign is chosen to match the physics:

- minus sign in the denominator when the source moves **towards** the observer ($f_{\text{o}} > f_{\text{s}}$),
- plus sign when the source moves **away** from the observer ($f_{\text{o}} < f_{\text{s}}$).

You are not required to handle a moving observer; assume the observer is stationary.

A worked example. A car emits a horn at $f_{\text{s}} = 800\ \text{Hz}$, moving at $30\ \text{m s}^{-1}$ towards a stationary listener. Speed of sound $v = 340\ \text{m s}^{-1}$:

$$
f_{\text{o}} = \frac{340 \times 800}{340 - 30} = \frac{272\,000}{310} \approx 877\ \text{Hz}.
$$

## Electromagnetic spectrum

All electromagnetic (EM) waves are **transverse** and travel in vacuum (free space) at the same speed:

$$
c = 3.00 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m s}^{-1}.
$$

This includes radio, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays and $\gamma$-rays.

Approximate wavelength ranges in free space (memorise the orders of magnitude):

- radio waves: $> 10^{-1}\ \text{m}$ (up to many km).
- microwaves: $10^{-3}\ \text{m}$ to $10^{-1}\ \text{m}$.
- infrared: $\sim 7 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$ to $10^{-3}\ \text{m}$.
- visible light: $400\ \text{nm}$ (violet) to $700\ \text{nm}$ (red), i.e. $4 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$ to $7 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$.
- ultraviolet: $\sim 10^{-8}\ \text{m}$ to $4 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$.
- X-rays: $\sim 10^{-11}\ \text{m}$ to $10^{-8}\ \text{m}$.
- $\gamma$-rays: $< 10^{-11}\ \text{m}$.

The boundaries between adjacent regions are not sharp. Apply $c = f\lambda$ to convert between wavelength and frequency.

Visible light has wavelengths $400$–$700\ \text{nm}$. Anything outside this range cannot be seen.

## Polarisation

**Polarisation** is the restriction of the oscillation of a transverse wave to a single plane.

- Only **transverse** waves can be polarised — the oscillation direction is perpendicular to the propagation direction, so different perpendicular planes are distinct possibilities.
- **Longitudinal** waves (sound) cannot be polarised — the oscillation is along the propagation direction, no other plane available.

This fact is sometimes used to test whether a wave is transverse or longitudinal: if it can be polarised, it must be transverse.

### Malus's law

Plane-polarised light of intensity $I_{0}$ passes through a polarising filter whose transmission axis is at angle $\theta$ to the plane of polarisation. The transmitted intensity is

$$
I = I_{0} \cos^{2}\theta.
$$

- $\theta = 0°$: filter aligned with polarisation, $I = I_{0}$, fully transmitted.
- $\theta = 90°$: filter perpendicular, $I = 0$, blocked completely.
- $\theta = 60°$: $I = I_{0} \cos^{2} 60° = I_{0} \cdot 0.25 = I_{0}/4$.

For two filters in series, apply Malus's law twice with the appropriate angle between each pair. Be careful about the angle reference frame at each stage — the polarisation direction after the first filter is along that filter's axis, and the second filter's angle is measured from there.

(You are not required to compute the effect of a polarising filter on an unpolarised wave.)
