## Concept of a magnetic field

A **magnetic field** is a region of space in which a moving charge (or a current) experiences a force. The field is produced by either:

- **moving charges** (typically a current in a wire), or
- **permanent magnets** (where the field arises from microscopic atomic currents/spin).

### Field lines

Conventions:

- Field lines point from **N** (north) to **S** (south) outside a magnet, and from S to N inside the magnet (so the lines form closed loops).
- Lines never cross.
- Density indicates strength: closer lines → stronger field.

Standard patterns to recognise:

- **Bar magnet** — curving lines from N to S outside, with the field strongest near the poles.
- **Long straight current-carrying wire** — concentric circles around the wire; direction given by the **right-hand grip rule** (thumb along the conventional current, fingers curl in the direction of the field).
- **Flat circular coil** — through the centre of the coil the field is perpendicular to its plane; the loop acts like a small bar magnet (the face from which field lines emerge is the N face).
- **Long solenoid** — field inside is approximately **uniform** along the axis, like a stretched bar magnet; outside it weakens rapidly. Field direction inside the solenoid: along the axis, with the N face determined by the right-hand grip rule applied to the windings.

A **ferrous (iron) core** placed inside a solenoid greatly **increases** the magnetic field, because the iron's atomic magnets align with the field and add to it. This is why electromagnets and transformers have iron cores.

## Force on a current-carrying conductor

A current $I$ flowing through a wire of length $L$ placed in a magnetic field of flux density $B$ experiences a force

$$
F = B I L \sin\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the wire and the field direction.

Maximum force: when the wire is **perpendicular** to the field ($\sin\theta = 1$), $F = BIL$.

Zero force: when the wire is **parallel** to the field ($\sin\theta = 0$).

### Definition of magnetic flux density

The above expression also **defines** the magnetic flux density $B$:

$$
B = \frac{F}{IL} \quad\text{when wire is at right angles to the field}.
$$

So $B$ is the **force per unit current per unit length** on a wire placed at right angles to the field. Unit: tesla, $\text{T} = \text{N A}^{-1}\ \text{m}^{-1}$.

### Direction — Fleming's left-hand rule

Use the **left hand**:

- **F**irst finger — direction of the **F**ield ($B$).
- **Se**cond finger — direction of the (conventional) **C**urrent ($I$).
- **Th**umb — direction of the resulting force (**Th**rust) on the wire.

Hold the three fingers mutually perpendicular. The thumb gives the force direction.

## Force on a moving charge

A point charge $Q$ moving with velocity $v$ through a magnetic field experiences a force

$$
F = B Q v \sin\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $v$ and $B$. Same direction rule (Fleming's left hand): the second finger represents the direction of motion of a **positive** charge — for a negative charge, reverse it.

When $v$ is **perpendicular** to $B$, $F = BQv$ is at maximum. When $v$ is **parallel** to $B$, $F = 0$.

### Circular motion in a uniform magnetic field

A charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform field experiences a force perpendicular to both $v$ and $B$. This force does **no work** (always perpendicular to motion) — kinetic energy and speed stay constant. The particle moves in a **circle**.

Set the magnetic force equal to the centripetal force:

$$
B Q v = \frac{m v^{2}}{r}, \qquad r = \frac{m v}{B Q}.
$$

So the radius depends on the particle's momentum $mv$ and on $BQ$. The period is

$$
T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi m}{B Q}.
$$

The period and frequency do **not depend on the particle's speed** — a faster particle moves in a larger circle but takes the same time per revolution.

If the velocity has a component along $B$, that component is unaffected (no force along $B$). The motion is a **helix** — circle perpendicular to $B$, drift along $B$.

### Hall effect

A slab of conducting material carrying current $I$ through its width $w$, in a magnetic field $B$ perpendicular to the current, develops a voltage across its faces — the **Hall voltage** $V_{\text{H}}$.

Derivation: moving charges in the slab feel a magnetic force $BQv_{\text{d}}$ perpendicular to their motion, where $v_{\text{d}}$ is the drift velocity. They accumulate on one face, creating an electric field $E$ that opposes further accumulation. Steady state when $eE = Bev_{\text{d}}$, so $E = B v_{\text{d}}$.

The Hall voltage is $V_{\text{H}} = E \cdot w$. The current is $I = n e v_{\text{d}} A$, where $A = w t$ is the cross-section (thickness $t$). Solving for $v_{\text{d}} = I/(n e w t)$ and substituting:

$$
V_{\text{H}} = \frac{B I}{n t q},
$$

where $q$ is the carrier charge (often $e$). Use this expression directly when asked to derive or use $V_{\text{H}}$.

A **Hall probe** uses this effect to measure $B$: pass a known current through a thin semiconductor slab, measure $V_{\text{H}}$ as you orient the probe; the maximum $V_{\text{H}}$ occurs when the slab is perpendicular to $B$, giving $B = V_{\text{H}} n t q / I$ (or just a direct reading after calibration).

### Velocity selector

A **velocity selector** uses crossed electric and magnetic fields to pass only particles of one specific speed.

Particles enter a region with $E$ (electric field) and $B$ (magnetic field) at right angles to each other and to the velocity. The electric force is $qE$ in the direction of $E$ (for positive charge); the magnetic force is $qvB$ perpendicular to both $v$ and $B$. By orienting them so that $qvB$ opposes $qE$, you get **no net force only for a specific speed**:

$$
qE = qvB \quad\Rightarrow\quad v = \frac{E}{B}.
$$

Particles at speed $E/B$ pass straight through. Faster ones experience a net magnetic force; slower ones a net electric force. Both are deflected away.

## Magnetic forces between current-carrying conductors

Two long parallel wires carrying currents $I_{1}$ and $I_{2}$ each sit in the magnetic field of the other. The force per unit length on either wire is in the direction of the other if the currents flow the **same way** (attraction), and away from the other if the currents flow in **opposite directions** (repulsion).

Using Fleming's left-hand rule on wire 2 in the field of wire 1: the field at wire 2 due to wire 1 wraps around wire 1; pointing the first finger in that direction at wire 2's location, and the second finger along $I_{2}$, the thumb gives the force direction.

**Parallel currents attract; antiparallel currents repel.** This is the basis of the SI definition of the ampere (no calculation required here).

## Electromagnetic induction

### Magnetic flux

The **magnetic flux** $\Phi$ through a flat area $A$, perpendicular to the magnetic flux density $B$, is

$$
\Phi = B A.
$$

If the area's normal is at angle $\theta$ to $B$, use the perpendicular component of $B$: $\Phi = B A \cos\theta$.

Unit: weber, $\text{Wb} = \text{T m}^{2}$.

For a coil of $N$ turns, the total **flux linkage** is

$$
N\Phi = N B A.
$$

### Faraday's and Lenz's laws

When the flux linkage through a circuit **changes**, an **electromotive force (e.m.f.)** is induced in the circuit. Two complementary statements:

**Faraday's law**: the magnitude of the induced e.m.f. is **equal to the rate of change of flux linkage**:

$$
|\varepsilon| = \frac{d(N\Phi)}{dt} = N\frac{d\Phi}{dt}.
$$

**Lenz's law**: the induced e.m.f. is in such a direction as to **oppose the change** producing it. This sign captures conservation of energy: if the induced current reinforced the change, energy could be created from nothing.

Combining the two:

$$
\varepsilon = -\frac{d(N\Phi)}{dt}.
$$

The minus sign is Lenz's law. In numerical questions the syllabus usually wants the **magnitude** of the e.m.f. and a separate statement of direction.

### What causes the flux change?

Three common causes:

- **Changing $B$** while area and orientation are fixed (e.g. moving a magnet near a coil).
- **Changing area** $A$ — a rod sliding along rails in a fixed field changes the loop's area.
- **Changing orientation** — a coil rotating in a field, as in an a.c. generator (next topic).

### Demonstrations

- **Moving a bar magnet into a coil**: a deflection is seen on a galvanometer in the coil's circuit. The direction reverses when the magnet is withdrawn (Lenz's law). A faster motion gives a larger reading (Faraday's law).
- **Two coils, with iron core**: switching the current in the primary on or off induces a brief e.m.f. in the secondary — this is the basis of the transformer.
- **Disc swinging in a field**: a copper disc swinging into a magnet's field is rapidly slowed — eddy currents are induced that oppose the motion (eddy-current braking, a Lenz's-law effect).

### Factors affecting the magnitude of induced e.m.f.

From $\varepsilon = N d\Phi/dt$ with $\Phi = BA$:

- larger $N$ — more turns in the coil,
- larger $B$ — a stronger magnet or larger current in the inducing circuit,
- larger $A$ — a wider loop,
- faster change — moving the magnet faster, switching the current faster, rotating the coil faster.

Each gives a larger induced e.m.f. proportionally.
