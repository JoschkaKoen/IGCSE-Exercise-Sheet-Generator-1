## Luminosity and radiant flux intensity

The **luminosity** $L$ of a star is the **total power of radiation** it emits — the total energy radiated per second across all wavelengths and in all directions. Unit: watt (W).

A star sits at the centre of a "sphere of light". At distance $d$, the light has spread over the surface of a sphere of radius $d$ and area $4\pi d^{2}$. The **radiant flux intensity** $F$ (power per unit area, also called the radiation flux or brightness) at distance $d$ is

$$
F = \frac{L}{4\pi d^{2}}.
$$

Unit: $\text{W m}^{-2}$. This is the **inverse-square law for flux**. Doubling the distance reduces the flux to one quarter; tripling reduces it to one ninth. Same form as electric field strength of a point charge, or the intensity from a point sound source.

A telescope on Earth measures the flux $F$ from a star. If we also know the luminosity $L$, we can solve for $d$:

$$
d = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi F}}.
$$

This is how astronomers infer distances to distant objects.

## Standard candles

A **standard candle** is an astronomical object whose **luminosity is known** from its type. Once you have identified such an object in a distant galaxy and measured the flux $F$ you receive from it on Earth, you can calculate its distance using $d = \sqrt{L/(4\pi F)}$.

Examples (you do not need to memorise these — just the concept):

- Cepheid variables (pulsating stars) — their pulsation period is tightly correlated with luminosity, so measuring the period tells you $L$.
- Type Ia supernovae — produced by a white dwarf reaching a critical mass and exploding; the explosion always has approximately the same peak luminosity.

The key point: **a standard candle gives you $L$ without needing to know the distance first**. With $L$ known and $F$ measured, $d$ follows.

Standard candles allow distances to **galaxies far beyond direct parallax** to be measured — important for testing cosmological models such as Hubble's law (below).

## Stellar surface temperature

### Wien's displacement law

A hot body emits a continuous spectrum (a blackbody spectrum) with a peak at a wavelength $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ that depends on its temperature. **Wien's displacement law**:

$$
\lambda_{\text{max}} \propto \frac{1}{T}, \quad\text{equivalently}\quad \lambda_{\text{max}} T = \text{constant}.
$$

The constant has value $b \approx 2.90 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m K}$ (you'll see the constant labelled $b$ in some sources).

Hotter stars have their emission peak at **shorter** wavelengths:

- A cool red star ($T \approx 3000\ \text{K}$) has $\lambda_{\text{max}} \approx 10^{-6}\ \text{m}$ (near-infrared) — appears red.
- The Sun ($T \approx 5800\ \text{K}$) peaks at $\lambda_{\text{max}} \approx 500\ \text{nm}$ — appears yellow-white.
- A hot blue-white star ($T \approx 20{,}000\ \text{K}$) peaks at $\lambda_{\text{max}} \approx 150\ \text{nm}$ — emits mostly ultraviolet.

Measuring $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ of a star's spectrum gives its surface temperature.

### Stefan–Boltzmann law

The total luminosity of a star, modelled as a blackbody sphere of radius $r$ and surface temperature $T$, is

$$
L = 4\pi \sigma r^{2} T^{4},
$$

where $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{W m}^{-2}\ \text{K}^{-4}$ is the **Stefan–Boltzmann constant**.

This is the Stefan–Boltzmann law: a star's luminosity is the surface area $4\pi r^{2}$ multiplied by $\sigma T^{4}$ (the power radiated per unit area at temperature $T$).

Two strong dependences:

- $L \propto r^{2}$ — a giant star with twice the radius is four times as luminous at the same temperature.
- $L \propto T^{4}$ — a star with twice the surface temperature is sixteen times as luminous at the same radius.

### Estimating a stellar radius

Combine Wien's law and the Stefan–Boltzmann law:

1. Measure $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ from the star's spectrum → find $T$ from Wien's law.
2. Determine $L$ — e.g. from the flux $F$ and the distance $d$ (perhaps obtained via a standard candle): $L = 4\pi d^{2} F$.
3. Use the Stefan–Boltzmann law to solve for $r$:

$$
r = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi \sigma T^{4}}}.
$$

This is how astronomers estimate radii of stars they cannot resolve directly with a telescope.

## Redshift, Hubble's law and the Big Bang

### Cosmological redshift

The spectral lines (emission and absorption) of light from distant galaxies are observed at **longer wavelengths than their known laboratory values**. Each line is shifted in proportion to its rest wavelength — the whole spectrum is stretched towards the red. This is **cosmological redshift**.

Interpretation (using the simple Doppler formula for sound, adapted): the galaxy is moving **away** from us. The fractional redshift is

$$
\frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda} \approx \frac{\Delta f}{f} \approx \frac{v}{c},
$$

valid for $v \ll c$. Here $\Delta\lambda = \lambda_{\text{observed}} - \lambda_{\text{emitted}}$, and $v$ is the radial velocity (positive when receding).

A worked example. Light from a star is emitted at $\lambda_{\text{em}} = 4.62 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$, observed at $\lambda_{\text{obs}} = 4.91 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$.

$$
\Delta\lambda = 4.91 \times 10^{-7} - 4.62 \times 10^{-7} = 0.29 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m},
$$

$$
v \approx \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda_{\text{em}}} c = \frac{0.29}{4.62} \times 3.00 \times 10^{8} \approx 1.9 \times 10^{7}\ \text{m s}^{-1}.
$$

The star is receding from Earth at about $19{,}000\ \text{km s}^{-1}$.

### Why redshift implies an expanding Universe

The observation that **almost every distant galaxy is redshifted** (a few nearby ones are blueshifted because of local motions) tells us that on average, galaxies are moving away from us. Not just from us — from every other galaxy too. The whole Universe is **expanding**, with the space between galaxies stretching over time.

More distant galaxies are redshifted more strongly. The redshift is not due to galaxies hurtling through pre-existing space, but to the **expansion of space itself** between them.

### Hubble's law

The observed relation between recession speed $v$ and distance $d$ is **Hubble's law**:

$$
v \approx H_{0} \cdot d,
$$

where $H_{0}$ is **Hubble's constant** (with units of $\text{s}^{-1}$ when $v$ is in m/s and $d$ in m). $H_{0}$ has been measured at approximately $2.3 \times 10^{-18}\ \text{s}^{-1}$, or equivalently about $70\ \text{km s}^{-1}$ per megaparsec — but the syllabus says "candidates will only be required to use SI units", so always use s⁻¹, m, m/s.

Worked example. A galaxy is observed to be receding at $v = 1.9 \times 10^{7}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$. Distance:

$$
d = \frac{v}{H_{0}} = \frac{1.9 \times 10^{7}}{2.3 \times 10^{-18}} \approx 8.3 \times 10^{24}\ \text{m}.
$$

### From Hubble's law to the Big Bang

Hubble's law implies that all the galaxies were once concentrated together. **Running the expansion backwards** in time, every distance shrinks. Tracing it back to $t = -1/H_{0}$, all distances would have been zero — the entire Universe condensed to a single point of enormous density and temperature. This is the basic argument for a "big bang" origin.

The age of the Universe (assuming uniform expansion at rate $H_{0}$) is approximately

$$
T_{\text{age}} \approx \frac{1}{H_{0}}.
$$

Substituting $H_{0} \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-18}\ \text{s}^{-1}$: $T_{\text{age}} \approx 4.3 \times 10^{17}\ \text{s} \approx 1.4 \times 10^{10}$ years — about **14 billion years**, in rough agreement with other estimates. The actual expansion has not been perfectly uniform, so the precise age requires a more sophisticated calculation, but the order of magnitude is right.

The expansion of the Universe, the redshift of distant galaxies, the cosmic microwave background, and the relative abundance of hydrogen and helium are the main pillars of evidence for the **Big Bang theory** — the idea that the Universe began in a hot, dense state and has been expanding and cooling ever since.

### Distance ladder summary

Astronomers determine distances by combining several methods:

1. **Parallax** (the Earth's orbit gives a baseline) — for nearby stars.
2. **Standard candles** (Cepheids, Type Ia supernovae) — for galaxies.
3. **Hubble's law** ($d = v/H_{0}$, where $v$ comes from redshift) — for very distant galaxies.

Each rung in this "distance ladder" is calibrated by the rung below.
