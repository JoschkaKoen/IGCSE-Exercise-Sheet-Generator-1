## Electric fields

An **electric field** is a region of space in which a charge experiences a force due to other charges. The **electric field strength** $E$ at a point is the **force per unit positive charge** acting on a small positive test charge placed there:

$$
E = \frac{F}{q}.
$$

Unit: $\text{N C}^{-1}$ (equivalently $\text{V m}^{-1}$, as we'll see).

$E$ is a **vector** with direction pointing the way the force on a **positive** test charge would act. A negative charge feels a force in the opposite direction.

The force on a charge $q$ in field $E$ is

$$
F = qE.
$$

If $q$ is negative, the force is opposite to the field direction.

### Field lines

Conventions for sketching:

- Field lines point in the direction of the force on a **positive** test charge.
- Lines start on **positive** charges and end on **negative** charges (or extend to infinity).
- Lines never cross.
- The **density** of lines indicates the strength: closer lines → stronger field.

Examples:

- **Isolated positive point charge**: radial lines pointing outwards from the charge.
- **Isolated negative point charge**: radial lines pointing inwards.
- **Two opposite point charges** (a dipole): lines curve from the positive charge to the negative one.
- **Two like point charges**: lines repel each other, with a zero-field point between them.
- **Uniform field between two parallel charged plates**: equally spaced parallel lines from + plate to − plate (slight curving at the edges — "edge effects").

## Uniform electric fields

Between two parallel conducting plates a distance $d$ apart with a potential difference $V$ between them, the field is **uniform** (apart from edge effects) and has magnitude

$$
E = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d} = \frac{V}{d}.
$$

The direction is from the higher-potential plate to the lower-potential plate. The unit $\text{V m}^{-1}$ comes directly from this expression; numerically it equals $\text{N C}^{-1}$.

### Motion of a charged particle in a uniform field

A charge $q$ in a uniform field experiences a **constant force** $F = qE$ and therefore a **constant acceleration** $a = F/m = qE/m$. This is analogous to a mass in a uniform gravitational field.

- A particle released **at rest** accelerates along the field direction (for a positive charge) or against it (for a negative charge), gaining kinetic energy as it travels.
- A particle entering the field at right angles to the field direction follows a **parabolic** path — uniform velocity perpendicular to the field, uniform acceleration parallel to the field. This is the same shape as a projectile in gravity.

For an electron entering between charged plates, the deflection at the far side of the plates can be found by combining SUVAT equations applied to the perpendicular direction with the time spent inside the field. This is how cathode-ray tubes used to steer the beam.

## Coulomb's law

For two **point charges** $Q_{1}$ and $Q_{2}$ separated by distance $r$ in free space, each exerts a force on the other of magnitude

$$
F = \frac{Q_{1} Q_{2}}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r^{2}}.
$$

- $\varepsilon_{0} = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{F m}^{-1}$ is the **permittivity of free space**.
- The constant $1/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0}) \approx 9.0 \times 10^{9}\ \text{N m}^{2}\ \text{C}^{-2}$.

The direction is along the line joining the two charges: **repulsive** for like charges, **attractive** for opposite charges.

### Spherical conductors as point charges

For a **spherical conductor** carrying total charge $Q$ (uniformly distributed by symmetry), the electric field at any point **outside** the sphere is the same as that of a point charge $Q$ at the sphere's centre. This lets you apply Coulomb's law and the point-charge field formula to spheres as well, provided the separation is measured from the centre.

For a point **inside** a hollow charged sphere, the field is zero (not required to derive — but consequences appear: a charged conductor is an equipotential, no field inside).

## Electric field due to a point charge

The electric field at distance $r$ from an isolated point charge $Q$ in free space is

$$
E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r^{2}}.
$$

This follows by placing a test charge $q$ at distance $r$ and dividing the Coulomb force $F$ by $q$.

$E$ points radially outwards from a positive $Q$, radially inwards towards a negative $Q$. It falls as $1/r^{2}$ — exactly like gravitational field strength, except that gravity is always attractive.

For multiple point charges, the resultant field is the **vector sum** of the individual fields.

## Electric potential

**Electric potential** $V$ at a point is the **work done per unit positive charge** in bringing a small positive test charge from infinity to that point:

$$
V = \frac{W}{q}.
$$

Unit: $\text{J C}^{-1} = \text{V}$.

The potential is defined as **zero at infinity**.

For a positive source charge, work must be done **against** the repulsion to bring a positive test charge in, so $V > 0$ everywhere outside (and infinite at the source).

For a negative source charge, the attraction does positive work on the incoming positive test charge — i.e. you would have to do negative work — so $V < 0$ everywhere outside.

### Electric potential due to a point charge

$$
V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r}.
$$

Note the $1/r$ dependence (compared with $1/r^{2}$ for field). $V$ is a **scalar**; for multiple charges, the total potential is the **algebraic sum**.

### Connection between field and potential

The electric field at a point equals the **negative of the potential gradient**:

$$
E = -\frac{dV}{dx}.
$$

In the uniform-field case between parallel plates, $V$ varies linearly with position, so $E = -\Delta V / \Delta d$ in magnitude, agreeing with the earlier formula. The minus sign indicates that the field points in the direction of **decreasing** potential.

For a point charge:

$$
V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r}, \qquad -\frac{dV}{dr} = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r^{2}} = E.
$$

A graph of $V$ vs $r$ for a positive point charge is a $1/r$ curve, approaching zero as $r \to \infty$ and tending to $+\infty$ as $r \to 0$. The field $E$ is the negative gradient of this curve at each point.

### Electric potential energy

A test charge $q$ placed at a point of potential $V$ has **electric potential energy**

$$
E_{\text{P}} = q V.
$$

For two point charges $Q$ and $q$ separated by $r$:

$$
E_{\text{P}} = \frac{Qq}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r}.
$$

- For **like** charges (both positive or both negative), $E_{\text{P}} > 0$ — the system has stored energy that would be released if the charges flew apart.
- For **opposite** charges (one positive, one negative), $E_{\text{P}} < 0$ — the system is bound and energy must be supplied to separate the charges.

This expression has no $r \to \infty$ minus-zero ambiguity: $E_{\text{P}} \to 0$ as $r \to \infty$, as expected.

### Worked-example pattern

A common problem: an electron orbits a positive nucleus of charge $+Ze$ at distance $r$. The Coulomb attraction provides the centripetal force:

$$
\frac{Z e^{2}}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r^{2}} = \frac{m_{e} v^{2}}{r}, \qquad v = \sqrt{\frac{Z e^{2}}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} m_{e} r}}.
$$

Total energy of the electron: kinetic $\tfrac{1}{2} m_{e} v^{2}$ plus potential energy $-Z e^{2}/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r)$. Substituting:

$$
E_{\text{total}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m_{e} v^{2} - \frac{Z e^{2}}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r} = -\frac{Z e^{2}}{8\pi\varepsilon_{0} r}.
$$

This is negative (bound state); the magnitude is half the magnitude of the potential energy.

### Gravitational versus electric

The two field theories are structurally similar:

| Quantity | Gravitational | Electric |
|---|---|---|
| Source | mass $M$ (always positive) | charge $Q$ (can be ±) |
| Field strength | $g = GM/r^{2}$ | $E = Q/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r^{2})$ |
| Force on test object | $F = mg$ | $F = qE$ |
| Potential | $\phi = -GM/r$ (always ≤ 0) | $V = Q/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r)$ (sign of $Q$) |
| PE of two | $E_{\text{P}} = -GMm/r$ | $E_{\text{P}} = Qq/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r)$ |
| Nature | always attractive | attractive or repulsive |

The negative sign in the gravitational potential / PE reflects that gravity is always attractive (the convention "$V = 0$ at infinity" then forces $V < 0$ everywhere else). The electric potential has the sign of the source charge.
