## Practical circuits

### e.m.f. and p.d.

The **electromotive force** (e.m.f.) $\varepsilon$ of a source is the **energy transferred to each unit of charge** by the source as it drives the charge around a complete circuit. Unit: volt.

The **potential difference** (p.d.) across a component is the **energy converted from electrical to other forms by each unit of charge** as it passes through that component.

Both are measured in volts; they differ in energy direction:

- e.m.f. — energy **input** to the circuit by the source (chemical → electrical in a battery, mechanical → electrical in a generator).
- p.d. — energy **output** from the electrical form to another form (electrical → thermal in a resistor, → light in a lamp, → kinetic in a motor).

### Internal resistance

A real source has some **internal resistance** $r$ — usually the resistance of the electrolyte in a cell, or the windings of a generator. When current $I$ flows, an internal p.d. of $Ir$ is "lost" inside the source, so the terminal p.d. across the external circuit is

$$
V_{\text{terminal}} = \varepsilon - I r.
$$

Consequences:

- **No current** (open circuit, $I = 0$): the terminal p.d. equals the e.m.f.
- **Larger current**: the terminal p.d. falls.
- **Short circuit** ($R_{\text{external}} \to 0$): $I = \varepsilon / r$, large current, all energy dissipated inside the source.

To measure internal resistance, vary the external resistance and plot $V_{\text{terminal}}$ against $I$: the line has $y$-intercept $\varepsilon$ and gradient $-r$.

The power delivered to the external load is $P_{\text{ext}} = V_{\text{terminal}} \cdot I = (\varepsilon - Ir) I$; the power dissipated inside is $P_{\text{int}} = I^{2} r$; the total power supplied by the source is $\varepsilon I$.

### Circuit symbols

You must recognise and draw the standard symbols listed in section 6 of the syllabus: cell, battery, switch, resistor, variable resistor, ammeter, voltmeter, lamp, diode (LED, semiconductor diode), capacitor, inductor, thermistor, light-dependent resistor (LDR), fuse, earth, junction. An ideal **ammeter** has zero resistance and is placed in series. An ideal **voltmeter** has infinite resistance and is placed in parallel.

## Kirchhoff's laws

### First law (junction rule)

At any junction in a circuit, **the sum of the currents flowing in equals the sum of the currents flowing out**. Equivalently, the algebraic sum of currents at a junction is zero.

This is a direct consequence of **conservation of charge** — charge cannot accumulate at a point in a steady-state circuit, so charge in per second must equal charge out per second.

For a junction with three wires: $I_{1} = I_{2} + I_{3}$ if currents 2 and 3 flow away from the junction and current 1 flows in.

### Second law (loop rule)

Around **any closed loop** in a circuit, **the sum of the e.m.f.s equals the sum of the p.d.s across components in that loop**.

This is a consequence of **conservation of energy**: as a unit of charge travels once around a closed loop, the energy it gains from sources (e.m.f.s) equals the energy it gives up to components (p.d.s).

Pick a direction round the loop, take e.m.f.s as positive when the loop direction goes from − to + of the source, and take p.d.s positive when the loop direction is the conventional current direction through the resistor.

### Combining resistors

**Resistors in series** carry the same current; the total p.d. is the sum of the individual p.d.s. From Kirchhoff's second law:

$$
\varepsilon = I R_{1} + I R_{2} + \ldots = I (R_{1} + R_{2} + \ldots),
$$

so $R_{\text{series}} = R_{1} + R_{2} + \ldots$.

**Resistors in parallel** have the same p.d.; the total current is the sum of the individual currents. From Kirchhoff's first law:

$$
I = \frac{V}{R_{1}} + \frac{V}{R_{2}} + \ldots = V \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}} + \frac{1}{R_{2}} + \ldots\right),
$$

so $\dfrac{1}{R_{\text{parallel}}} = \dfrac{1}{R_{1}} + \dfrac{1}{R_{2}} + \ldots$.

For two identical resistors $R$ in parallel, the combined resistance is $R/2$. For $N$ identical $R$ in parallel, combined resistance is $R/N$.

A parallel combination is **always smaller** than any of its component resistances; a series combination is always larger than any of its components.

### Solving a circuit

A general recipe:

1. **Label** every distinct current with a symbol and a chosen direction.
2. **Apply Kirchhoff's first law** at each junction to relate the currents (often one independent equation per junction beyond the first).
3. **Apply Kirchhoff's second law** around each independent loop to get equations involving p.d.s.
4. **Use $V = IR$** for each resistor to convert p.d.s into the currents you labelled.
5. Solve simultaneously.

For symmetric resistor networks (six identical resistors arranged as on a frame, etc.), exploit the symmetry to identify branches carrying equal currents — the most-current branch dissipates the most power ($P = I^{2}R$).

## Potential dividers

A **potential divider** is two (or more) resistors in series across a source. The p.d. across each resistor is in **direct proportion** to its resistance:

$$
V_{1} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \frac{R_{1}}{R_{1} + R_{2}}, \qquad V_{2} = V_{\text{in}} \cdot \frac{R_{2}}{R_{1} + R_{2}}.
$$

The output (tapped between $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$) can be made any voltage between $0$ and $V_{\text{in}}$ by adjusting the resistances. A **rheostat** or **slider** on a uniform-resistance wire provides a continuously variable potential divider.

### Sensor circuits

Replace one of the fixed resistors in a potential divider with a sensor whose resistance changes with a physical quantity:

- **Thermistor** (NTC): $R$ decreases as temperature rises. In a potential divider, the output voltage changes with temperature in a known direction. With the thermistor in the upper arm, $V_{\text{out}}$ across the fixed resistor in the lower arm **increases** as temperature rises (the divider gives a larger share to the lower arm as the upper arm's resistance falls).
- **LDR**: $R$ decreases as light intensity rises. Same logic gives a brightness-dependent output voltage.

Connect the output to a transistor base or a comparator to switch a load on or off when the temperature/light crosses a threshold.

### Potentiometer and null method

A **potentiometer** is a uniform resistance wire of length $L_{0}$, with a sliding contact (jockey). The wire has uniform resistance per unit length, so the p.d. between one end and the jockey is proportional to the length:

$$
V_{x} = V_{\text{full}} \cdot \frac{x}{L_{0}}.
$$

To **compare two e.m.f.s** (an unknown cell against a standard cell), connect each cell in turn with the jockey through a galvanometer. Slide the jockey until the galvanometer reads zero (a **null** — no current flows through the cell being measured because the potentiometer's voltage at that point exactly opposes the cell's e.m.f.). The two balance lengths $l_{1}, l_{2}$ are in the ratio of the e.m.f.s:

$$
\frac{\varepsilon_{1}}{\varepsilon_{2}} = \frac{l_{1}}{l_{2}}.
$$

A **galvanometer** is a sensitive current detector — used here in a **null method**, where the goal is to find the balance condition (zero current) rather than to measure a current's actual value. The advantage of a null method is that the unknown cell delivers no current at balance, so its internal resistance does not affect the result.
