## The nuclear atom

### Geiger–Marsden α-particle scattering

Alpha particles fired at a thin gold foil were observed to:

- mostly pass straight through, with very little deflection,
- occasionally be deflected through small angles,
- rarely (about $1$ in $8000$) be deflected through angles greater than $90°$.

These results led Rutherford to infer:

- the atom is **mostly empty space** (most $\alpha$-particles pass through unaffected),
- there is a **tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus** at the centre (the rare large-angle deflections require a concentrated charge to repel the $\alpha$),
- almost all of the atom's mass is concentrated in this nucleus.

Order of magnitude: atomic diameter $\sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$, nuclear diameter $\sim 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$ — the nucleus is about $10^{5}$ times smaller than the atom.

### Simple nuclear model

An atom contains:

- a central **nucleus** of **protons** (positive, charge $+e$) and **neutrons** (neutral, no charge),
- **electrons** (charge $-e$) in orbits around the nucleus.

The proton and neutron have almost the same mass ($\approx 1\ \text{u}$); the electron is about $\tfrac{1}{1836}$ as massive as the proton.

### Notation and key numbers

For a nuclide written as $^{A}_{Z}\text{X}$:

- **proton number** $Z$ (also called atomic number): the number of protons in the nucleus. Determines the element.
- **nucleon number** $A$ (also called mass number): the total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.
- number of **neutrons** $N = A - Z$.

A neutral atom has the same number of electrons as protons.

### Isotopes

**Isotopes** are atoms of the same element (same $Z$) with different numbers of neutrons (different $A$). They have the same chemical behaviour but different nuclear behaviour (different masses, different decay properties). Example: $^{12}_{6}\text{C}$ and $^{14}_{6}\text{C}$ are isotopes of carbon.

### Conservation laws in nuclear processes

In any nuclear process:

- **nucleon number** $A$ is conserved (total $A$ before $=$ total $A$ after),
- **charge** is conserved (total $Z$ before $=$ total $Z$ after, when each particle contributes its charge as a multiple of $e$).

These two laws let you balance decay and reaction equations.

### Unified atomic mass unit

The **unified atomic mass unit**, symbol $\text{u}$, is defined so that an atom of $^{12}_{6}\text{C}$ has mass exactly $12\ \text{u}$. Numerically,

$$
1\ \text{u} = 1.661 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}.
$$

A proton has mass $\approx 1.007\ \text{u}$; a neutron $\approx 1.009\ \text{u}$; an electron $\approx 5.5 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{u}$.

## Radioactive emissions

A nucleus that is unstable rearranges itself, emitting one of three kinds of radiation. Each has distinct properties.

### α-radiation

- **Composition**: a helium-4 nucleus, $^{4}_{2}\alpha$ (two protons + two neutrons).
- **Mass**: $\approx 4\ \text{u}$ (large compared to $\beta$).
- **Charge**: $+2e$.
- **Range in air**: a few cm. Stopped by a sheet of paper.
- **Ionising power**: strong (it interacts heavily because of mass and charge).
- **Energy spectrum**: **discrete** (a given decay process emits $\alpha$-particles at one or a few sharply defined energies).

### β-radiation

There are two types:

- $\beta^{-}$: a **fast-moving electron** emitted from the nucleus when a neutron decays into a proton.
- $\beta^{+}$: a **positron** (the electron's antiparticle) emitted when a proton in a proton-rich nucleus decays into a neutron.

Properties (both types):

- **Mass**: $\approx 1/1836\ \text{u}$ (much less than $\alpha$).
- **Charge**: $-e$ for $\beta^{-}$, $+e$ for $\beta^{+}$.
- **Range in air**: about $1\ \text{m}$. Stopped by a few millimetres of aluminium.
- **Energy spectrum**: **continuous** range up to a maximum, because each $\beta$-decay also releases an (anti)neutrino that carries away a variable share of the available energy. (See "Why β is continuous" below.)

### γ-radiation

- **Composition**: a high-energy photon — part of the electromagnetic spectrum.
- **Mass**: zero (rest mass).
- **Charge**: zero.
- **Range in air**: large (follows the inverse-square law, never absolutely stopped). Significantly attenuated by several cm of lead or about a metre of concrete.
- **Ionising power**: weakest.
- **Energy spectrum**: discrete (the nucleus emits a photon as it falls between two nuclear energy levels).

A nucleus often emits a γ-photon as a "tidy-up" step after an α or β decay leaves the daughter nucleus in an excited state.

### Antiparticles, neutrinos and antineutrinos

Every particle has an **antiparticle** with the **same mass** but **opposite charge** (and other quantum numbers reversed). The positron $\text{e}^{+}$ is the antiparticle of the electron $\text{e}^{-}$.

In $\beta$-decay, a third particle is always emitted alongside the electron or positron:

- $\beta^{-}$ decay: an **(electron) antineutrino** $\bar{\nu}_{\text{e}}$ is also emitted.
- $\beta^{+}$ decay: an **(electron) neutrino** $\nu_{\text{e}}$ is also emitted.

Neutrinos and antineutrinos have zero charge, very small mass, and barely interact — they are extremely difficult to detect directly but their emission is required to balance energy, momentum and other conservation laws in $\beta$-decay.

### Why β has a continuous spectrum (and α does not)

In α-decay, the energy released is shared between just two particles (the daughter nucleus and the $\alpha$). Conservation of momentum and energy then fixes the $\alpha$'s energy to a discrete value.

In β-decay, the energy released is shared between **three** particles (the daughter nucleus, the $\beta$, and the (anti)neutrino). The $\beta$ can take any fraction of the total energy from zero up to a maximum, with the rest going to the (anti)neutrino. So the observed $\beta$ energy spectrum is continuous up to a maximum.

### Writing decay equations

A general α-decay:

$$
^{A}_{Z}\text{X} \to {}^{A-4}_{Z-2}\text{Y} + {}^{4}_{2}\alpha
$$

(check: nucleon number $A = (A-4) + 4$, charge $Z = (Z-2) + 2$.)

A general $\beta^{-}$ decay:

$$
^{A}_{Z}\text{X} \to {}^{A}_{Z+1}\text{Y} + {}^{0}_{-1}\beta + \bar{\nu}_{\text{e}}.
$$

A general $\beta^{+}$ decay:

$$
^{A}_{Z}\text{X} \to {}^{A}_{Z-1}\text{Y} + {}^{0}_{+1}\beta + \nu_{\text{e}}.
$$

## Fundamental particles

Some particles are **fundamental** (point-like, no internal structure as far as we know); others are made of fundamentals.

### Quarks

A **quark** is a fundamental particle. There are six flavours:

- **up** (u), charge $+\tfrac{2}{3}e$,
- **down** (d), charge $-\tfrac{1}{3}e$,
- **charm** (c), charge $+\tfrac{2}{3}e$,
- **strange** (s), charge $-\tfrac{1}{3}e$,
- **top** (t), charge $+\tfrac{2}{3}e$,
- **bottom** (b), charge $-\tfrac{1}{3}e$.

Each quark has an antiquark with **the same magnitude charge but opposite sign**. Antiquarks are written with a bar: $\bar{u}$ (charge $-\tfrac{2}{3}e$), $\bar{d}$ (charge $+\tfrac{1}{3}e$).

No other quark property is examined.

### Hadrons: baryons and mesons

Particles built from quarks are called **hadrons**. They come in two types:

- **Baryons** — three quarks. Examples: proton (u u d), neutron (u d d). Charges check out: $\tfrac{2}{3} + \tfrac{2}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} = +1$ for the proton; $\tfrac{2}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} = 0$ for the neutron.
- **Mesons** — one quark and one antiquark. (Examples not required, but $\pi^{+}$ is u$\bar{\text{d}}$.)

Protons and neutrons are **not** fundamental — they are baryons built of quarks.

### Quark transitions in β-decay

In $\beta^{-}$ decay, a neutron turns into a proton; in quark terms, **one down quark turns into an up quark**:

$$
\text{d} \to \text{u} + \beta^{-} + \bar{\nu}_{\text{e}}.
$$

In $\beta^{+}$ decay, a proton turns into a neutron; **one up quark turns into a down quark**:

$$
\text{u} \to \text{d} + \beta^{+} + \nu_{\text{e}}.
$$

(Check: in $\beta^{-}$ decay, charge of u $-$ charge of d $= \tfrac{2}{3} - (-\tfrac{1}{3}) = 1$, matched by the $-1$ of the $\beta^{-}$; charges balance.)

### Leptons

**Leptons** are fundamental particles that are not made of quarks. Electrons and neutrinos are leptons. (Heavier leptons exist — muon, tau — but are not required for this syllabus.)

### Quick particle classifier

When asked "which list contains only fundamental particles?": **quarks, electrons, positrons, neutrinos, antineutrinos** are fundamental; protons, neutrons, baryons, mesons, hadrons are not.
