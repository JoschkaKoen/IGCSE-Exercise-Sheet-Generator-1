## Turning effects of forces

### Centre of gravity

The **weight** of an extended object may be treated as acting at a single point called the **centre of gravity** (centre of mass for a body in a uniform gravitational field). For a uniform symmetric object — a rectangle, a sphere, a uniform rod — the centre of gravity is at the geometric centre.

When you draw a free-body diagram, always place the weight arrow at the centre of gravity.

### Moment of a force

The **moment** of a force about a point is

$$
M = F \cdot d,
$$

where $F$ is the magnitude of the force and $d$ is the **perpendicular distance** from the point to the line of action of the force. Unit: $\text{N m}$.

If the force is applied at angle $\theta$ to a lever arm of length $r$ measured from the pivot, then $d = r\sin\theta$, so $M = F r\sin\theta$. Only the component of the force **perpendicular to the lever** contributes a turning effect.

A moment acts either clockwise or anticlockwise about the chosen point.

### Couple and torque

A **couple** is a pair of forces that are:

- **equal in magnitude**,
- **opposite in direction**,
- with their lines of action **separated by a perpendicular distance**.

A couple produces **rotation only** — its resultant force is zero, so it gives no linear acceleration to the body.

The **torque of a couple** is the moment that a couple produces:

$$
\tau = F \cdot d,
$$

where $F$ is the magnitude of one of the forces and $d$ is the perpendicular distance between the two lines of action. Unit: $\text{N m}$. The torque is the same whatever point you take moments about — that's a special property of couples.

A common multiple-choice trap: forces of equal magnitude acting in the **same** direction are not a couple (they have a non-zero resultant and produce a net translation). Both equal magnitude *and* opposite direction are required.

## Equilibrium of forces

### Conditions for equilibrium

A body is in **equilibrium** when:

1. the **resultant force** is zero (no linear acceleration), AND
2. the **resultant moment** (or torque) about any point is zero (no angular acceleration).

Both conditions must hold. A body with no resultant force can still be rotating; a body with no resultant moment can still be translating.

### Principle of moments

For a body in rotational equilibrium, **the sum of the clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of the anticlockwise moments about the same point**. This is the **principle of moments**.

When solving a balance problem:

1. Choose a pivot — usually the one with an unknown force acting through it, so that force drops out (its moment arm is zero).
2. List every force and its perpendicular distance from the pivot.
3. Set $\sum M_{\text{clockwise}} = \sum M_{\text{anticlockwise}}$.
4. Use $\sum F = 0$ if you need a second equation.

A ruler balanced on a fulcrum with masses on either side is solved this way. For a heavy uniform rod, remember to include its weight acting at its centre of gravity.

### Vector triangle

Three coplanar forces in equilibrium can be represented by a **closed vector triangle** — drawn tip-to-tail, the three vectors return to the start point. This is a graphical alternative to resolving into components.

Use the **sine rule** or the **cosine rule** on the triangle to find unknown magnitudes or directions, or build the triangle to scale on graph paper.

If the forces are at angles to one another, you can also resolve each into horizontal and vertical components and set $\sum F_{x} = 0$ and $\sum F_{y} = 0$.

## Density

**Density** is the mass per unit volume:

$$
\rho = \frac{m}{V}.
$$

Unit: $\text{kg m}^{-3}$ (or $\text{g cm}^{-3}$; $1\ \text{g cm}^{-3} = 1000\ \text{kg m}^{-3}$). Density is a scalar.

Some useful densities to recognise:

- water: $1000\ \text{kg m}^{-3}$
- air at room conditions: $\sim 1.2\ \text{kg m}^{-3}$
- iron / steel: $\sim 7800\ \text{kg m}^{-3}$

## Pressure

**Pressure** is the force per unit area, where the force acts perpendicular to the surface:

$$
p = \frac{F}{A}.
$$

Unit: $\text{Pa} = \text{N m}^{-2}$. Pressure is a scalar.

### Hydrostatic pressure

Consider a column of fluid of density $\rho$, cross-sectional area $A$ and height $\Delta h$. Its weight is

$$
W = m g = (\rho \cdot A \cdot \Delta h) \cdot g.
$$

This weight presses down on the area $A$ at the bottom, so the **extra pressure** at the bottom compared to the top is

$$
\Delta p = \frac{W}{A} = \rho g \Delta h.
$$

This is the **hydrostatic pressure** equation. It depends only on the density of the fluid and the vertical depth — the shape of the container does not matter.

For a submarine at depth $h$ below the surface, the pressure due to the water column is $\rho_{\text{seawater}}\, g\, h$. To get the **total pressure**, add atmospheric pressure at the surface (about $1.0 \times 10^{5}\ \text{Pa}$).

### Upthrust and Archimedes' principle

When an object is submerged in a fluid, the **pressure at the bottom of the object is greater than the pressure at the top** (by $\rho g \Delta h$, where $\Delta h$ is the object's height). This pressure difference produces a net upwards force called the **upthrust**.

For an object of volume $V$ (the volume actually displaced from the fluid), the upthrust is

$$
F_{\text{upthrust}} = \rho_{\text{fluid}}\, g\, V.
$$

This is **Archimedes' principle**: the upthrust on a body in a fluid equals the weight of the fluid displaced.

For a fully submerged object, $V$ is the object's full volume. For a floating object, $V$ is the volume of the part **below the fluid surface** — the object floats when the upthrust on its submerged portion equals its weight.

### Force balance with upthrust

A block held under water by a string attached to the container bottom is in equilibrium under three vertical forces: weight (down), tension (down), upthrust (up). Set $F_{\text{upthrust}} = W + T$ to find the tension.

A submerged block hanging from a newton meter reads less than its weight in air because of the upthrust: reading $= W - F_{\text{upthrust}}$.

The upthrust depends on the **fluid density** and the **displaced volume**, not on the object's material or depth (for an incompressible fluid). On a planet with smaller $g$, the upthrust is smaller in the same ratio as the weight, so a floating object still floats with the same fraction submerged.
