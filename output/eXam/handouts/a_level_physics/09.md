## Electric current

An **electric current** is a flow of **charge carriers**. In a metal the carriers are negatively charged conduction electrons; in an electrolyte they are positive and negative ions; in a semiconductor they may be electrons or "holes". The conventional current direction is the direction in which **positive** charge would flow — opposite to the actual flow of electrons in a wire.

### Charge

Charge is **quantised**: the smallest free unit of charge is the elementary charge

$$
e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}.
$$

Every free charge in this syllabus carries an integer multiple of $e$. The unit of charge is the **coulomb**, $\text{C}$.

### Current as rate of flow of charge

If charge $Q$ passes a point in time $t$, the current is

$$
I = \frac{Q}{t}, \qquad Q = It.
$$

Unit of current: ampere, $\text{A}$ ($= \text{C s}^{-1}$). Current is a scalar in the syllabus treatment, though direction along the conductor must be tracked.

For a non-steady current, the charge that has flowed in a given time interval is the **area under** an $I$–$t$ graph.

### Drift velocity expression

For a uniform conductor of cross-section area $A$, with $n$ charge carriers per unit volume (the "number density"), each carrying charge $q$, drifting with average velocity $v$ along the conductor:

$$
I = A n v q.
$$

Use this to compare currents in different materials or different parts of a circuit:

- Reducing $A$ (a thinner wire) at constant $I$ requires faster drift $v$.
- A semiconductor has many orders of magnitude fewer free carriers than a metal (smaller $n$), so for the same $I$ the drift velocity is much larger.
- In a circuit with series components, $I$ is the same everywhere, so $nvq$ adjusts: if $A$ is the same but the material changes, then $nv$ changes inversely.

## Potential difference

The **potential difference** (p.d.) across a component is the **energy transferred per unit charge** as that charge passes through the component:

$$
V = \frac{W}{Q}.
$$

Unit: volt, $\text{V}$ ($= \text{J C}^{-1}$).

If $1\ \text{J}$ of electrical energy is converted to other forms (thermal, light, kinetic, etc.) when $1\ \text{C}$ of charge passes through a component, then the p.d. across that component is $1\ \text{V}$.

The **electromotive force** (e.m.f.) of a source is the energy supplied **per unit charge by the source**. Numerically e.m.f. and p.d. have the same definition; the distinction is energy direction: e.m.f. is energy given to the charge by the source; p.d. is energy given up by the charge to the component.

## Electrical power

Combining $V = W/Q$ and $I = Q/t$:

$$
P = \frac{W}{t} = V I.
$$

Using Ohm's law $V = IR$ in either direction:

$$
P = V I = I^{2} R = \frac{V^{2}}{R}.
$$

Pick whichever form contains the quantities you already know. Examples:

- two heating elements with the same resistance — the one with the larger current dissipates more power ($P = I^{2}R$).
- two resistors in parallel across the same voltage — the one with smaller $R$ dissipates more power ($P = V^{2}/R$).
- a kettle rated "$2.4\ \text{kW}, 240\ \text{V}$" draws $I = P/V = 10\ \text{A}$ and has resistance $R = V^{2}/P = 24\ \Omega$.

Energy transferred in time $t$ is $E = P t$.

## Resistance and Ohm's law

The **resistance** $R$ of a component is defined as

$$
R = \frac{V}{I}.
$$

Unit: ohm, $\Omega$ ($= \text{V A}^{-1}$). Resistance is a property of a component at the conditions (temperature, etc.) of the measurement.

### Ohm's law

A conductor obeys **Ohm's law** when the current through it is **proportional to the potential difference** across it, provided the physical conditions (temperature in particular) remain constant. For an ohmic conductor, $R$ is constant and the $I$–$V$ graph is a straight line through the origin.

Ohm's law is an experimental statement, not a definition. The definition of resistance $R = V/I$ applies to **any** component; only ohmic components have constant $R$.

### $I$–$V$ characteristics

You should be able to sketch these:

- **Metallic conductor at constant temperature** — straight line through the origin (ohmic). The line has positive gradient $1/R$ in the $I$ vs $V$ form; if reversed, current still flows the opposite way, giving a straight line through the origin in both quadrants.
- **Filament lamp** — passes through the origin with a steep initial gradient that flattens as $V$ (and hence $I$) increases. Reason: as current increases, the filament heats up, so its resistance rises and the gradient ($1/R$) falls. Not ohmic.
- **Semiconductor diode** — almost no current for negative $V$ or for small positive $V$. Above a "switch-on" voltage (about $0.7\ \text{V}$ for a silicon diode), current rises sharply with $V$. Highly non-ohmic and asymmetric.

### Resistivity

For a uniform conductor of length $L$ and cross-section area $A$, the resistance is

$$
R = \frac{\rho L}{A}.
$$

$\rho$ is the **resistivity**, a property of the material with unit $\Omega\ \text{m}$. Doubling the length doubles the resistance; doubling the area halves it; using a wire of half the diameter quarters the area and so quadruples $R$.

Typical values: copper at room temperature $\rho \sim 1.7 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\ \text{m}$; an insulator $\rho \sim 10^{15}\ \Omega\ \text{m}$ or larger.

The resistivity of metals **increases with temperature** (more lattice vibration scatters the electrons), explaining the curving $I$–$V$ characteristic of the filament lamp.

## Light-dependent resistor (LDR)

An LDR is a semiconductor whose **resistance decreases as light intensity increases**. In bright light $R$ may be a few hundred $\Omega$; in darkness it can be in the megaohms. LDRs are used in light-sensitive circuits (street-lamp switching, camera light meters).

## Thermistor

A **thermistor** in this syllabus is taken to be of the negative-temperature-coefficient type: **its resistance decreases as its temperature increases**. Useful for temperature sensing — connect in a potential divider and the output voltage shifts with temperature.

The decrease in resistance with temperature is the opposite of a metal — in a semiconductor, more thermal energy frees more charge carriers, which dominates over the increased scattering.
