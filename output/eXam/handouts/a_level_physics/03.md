## Mass, momentum and force

### Mass

**Mass** is the property of an object that resists changes in its motion. The larger the mass, the larger the force needed to produce a given acceleration. Mass is measured in kilograms ($\text{kg}$) and is a scalar.

### Linear momentum

**Linear momentum** is defined as the product of mass and velocity:

$$
p = mv.
$$

Momentum is a vector — it carries the direction of the velocity. Unit: $\text{kg m s}^{-1}$, equivalently $\text{N s}$.

### Force as the rate of change of momentum

Newton's second law in its general form: **the resultant force on an object is equal to the rate of change of its momentum**.

$$
F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}.
$$

When mass is constant this reduces to $F = ma$, because $\Delta p = m\,\Delta v$ and $\Delta v / \Delta t = a$. Cambridge questions often ask you to use $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$ directly when there is a collision or impulse: the **average force** equals the change in momentum divided by the contact time.

For a ball that hits a wall with momentum $p_{1}$ and rebounds with momentum $p_{2}$ in the opposite direction, the change in momentum is $\Delta p = p_{2} - p_{1}$ (treat each direction with the correct sign). The average force is $\Delta p / \Delta t$ where $\Delta t$ is the contact time.

The change in kinetic energy in terms of momentum, useful when $p$ is given rather than $v$, is

$$
\Delta E_{\text{k}} = \frac{p_{2}^{2} - p_{1}^{2}}{2m}.
$$

This follows from $E_{\text{k}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2} = p^{2} / (2m)$.

## Newton's three laws of motion

### First law

An object continues at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line **unless acted on by a resultant external force**. Equivalent statement: zero resultant force implies zero acceleration (constant velocity or rest).

### Second law

The **resultant force** on an object is proportional to its rate of change of momentum, and acts in the same direction as the change. In SI units,

$$
F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = ma \quad\text{(for constant mass)}.
$$

Acceleration and resultant force are **always in the same direction**.

### Third law

When body A exerts a force on body B, body B exerts an **equal and opposite** force on body A. The two forces

- act on **different objects**,
- are of the **same type** (both gravitational, both contact, both electrostatic, etc.),
- have the same magnitude and opposite direction.

A common trap: weight and the normal contact force on a block resting on a table are **not** a third-law pair (they act on the same object and are of different types). The third-law partner of the block's weight is the gravitational pull the block exerts on the Earth. The third-law partner of the contact force from the table is the contact force the block exerts on the table.

For a rocket: the thrust on the rocket and the force on the gases are a third-law pair (engine pushes gas down, gas pushes engine up). Weight and air resistance are not part of this pair.

## Weight

**Weight** is the force on an object due to a gravitational field. Near the Earth's surface,

$$
W = mg,
$$

where $g \approx 9.81\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ is the acceleration of free fall. Weight is a vector pointing towards the centre of the Earth. Do not confuse it with mass: mass is intrinsic and the same everywhere; weight changes with location.

## Non-uniform motion: friction, drag and terminal velocity

### Friction and viscous (drag) forces

A **frictional force** between solid surfaces opposes relative motion and acts parallel to the surface in contact. A **viscous** or **drag** force is the resistive force of a fluid (liquid or gas) on a body moving through it; "air resistance" is the special case for air. You do not need any coefficient of friction or viscosity.

A simple model: drag increases as speed increases. At zero speed, drag is zero.

### Object falling through air

For an object released from rest and falling through air:

1. Initially, only weight acts, so the object accelerates downwards at $g$.
2. As speed increases, drag (acting upwards) grows. The **resultant force** decreases, so acceleration decreases.
3. Eventually drag equals weight in magnitude. The resultant force is zero, the acceleration is zero, and the speed becomes constant — the **terminal velocity**.

On a velocity–time graph, the curve starts as a straight line with gradient $g$, then bends and levels off at the terminal velocity. This pattern (high initial acceleration, decreasing acceleration, then constant velocity) is what distinguishes "falling with air resistance" from "free fall in a vacuum".

### Energy during terminal-velocity fall

At terminal velocity the parachutist's kinetic energy is constant. Gravitational potential energy is still decreasing as the parachutist descends. Where does it go? Almost all of it becomes **thermal energy of the surrounding air** (and a small amount of thermal energy and kinetic energy of the air pushed aside). It does **not** go into kinetic energy of the parachutist — kinetic energy is constant.

### Cyclist or car at constant speed

A vehicle moving at constant speed on a level road has zero resultant force. The forward driving force is equal and opposite to the total resistive force (friction at bearings, air resistance, rolling resistance). At higher speed, the driving force must be larger to balance the larger drag — so the power output must increase.

## Conservation of linear momentum

### Principle

For a system with no external resultant force, the **total linear momentum is constant**.

This is the **principle of conservation of momentum**. It always holds in the absence of external forces — in collisions, explosions, recoils, and in 2-D problems where momentum is conserved separately along each perpendicular axis.

### Elastic vs inelastic collisions

In **every** collision, momentum is conserved (if no external resultant force).

An **elastic** collision is one in which **total kinetic energy is also conserved**. Equivalent test: in an elastic collision the **relative speed of approach equals the relative speed of separation**.

In an **inelastic** collision, momentum is conserved but **kinetic energy decreases** — some energy becomes thermal, sound, or deformation energy. A perfectly inelastic collision is the limiting case where the two objects stick together afterwards.

### Solving collision problems (1-D)

For two objects with masses $m_{1}, m_{2}$ and initial velocities $u_{1}, u_{2}$ that collide head-on, write

$$
m_{1} u_{1} + m_{2} u_{2} = m_{1} v_{1} + m_{2} v_{2}.
$$

Use **signed** velocities (positive in one chosen direction). If the collision is elastic, add the relative-speed equation

$$
u_{1} - u_{2} = -(v_{1} - v_{2}),
$$

or equivalently $\tfrac{1}{2} m_{1} u_{1}^{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} m_{2} u_{2}^{2} = \tfrac{1}{2} m_{1} v_{1}^{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} m_{2} v_{2}^{2}$. Two equations, two unknowns.

A useful result for a head-on elastic collision of mass $m$ with stationary mass $M$:

$$
v_{m} = \frac{m - M}{m + M} u, \qquad v_{M} = \frac{2m}{m + M} u.
$$

### Two-dimensional collisions

If the objects move in 2-D, **resolve velocities into perpendicular components** and apply conservation of momentum **independently** along each axis. For example, in a glancing collision pick axes along the initial motion and perpendicular to it. The total $x$-component of momentum is conserved; the total $y$-component is conserved.

### Rocket / ejecting mass

A rocket ejecting gas at velocity $u$ relative to itself, at a mass-flow rate $\dot m$ (kg per second), experiences a thrust

$$
F = \dot m \cdot u
$$

obtained from $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$. The change in momentum per second of the ejected gas equals the thrust on the rocket (Newton's third law: rocket pushes gas down, gas pushes rocket up).
