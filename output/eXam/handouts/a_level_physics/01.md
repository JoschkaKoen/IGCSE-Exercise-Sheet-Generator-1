## Physical quantities

A **physical quantity** always has two parts: a **numerical magnitude** and a **unit**. The number alone — like 5.2 — tells you nothing physical. You need to know what is being measured (length, time, mass, …) and the unit it is measured in.

When you state a quantity, you must give both. "The length is 1.5" is incomplete. "The length is 1.5 m" is a physical quantity.

### Reasonable estimates

You are expected to make **reasonable estimates** of physical quantities you meet in this syllabus. Some useful rough values to memorise:

- mass of an adult human: $\sim 70\ \text{kg}$
- weight of an adult human: $\sim 700\ \text{N}$
- height of an adult human: $\sim 1.7\ \text{m}$
- mass of an apple: $\sim 0.1\ \text{kg}$ (so weight $\sim 1\ \text{N}$)
- speed of sound in air: $\sim 340\ \text{m s}^{-1}$
- speed of light in vacuum: $3.0 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$
- acceleration of free fall: $g \approx 9.81\ \text{m s}^{-2}$

A good estimate is "of the right order of magnitude". A human mass of 70 kg is reasonable; 7 kg is not.

## SI units

### Base units

There are five SI base quantities you must recall, along with their units:

- **mass** — kilogram, $\text{kg}$
- **length** — metre, $\text{m}$
- **time** — second, $\text{s}$
- **current** — ampere, $\text{A}$
- **temperature** — kelvin, $\text{K}$

Every other unit in this syllabus is built from these five.

### Derived units

A **derived unit** is a product or quotient of base units. You should be able to express any quantity in the syllabus in terms of its base units.

Build derived units from the defining equation:

- **speed** = displacement / time, so units are $\text{m s}^{-1}$
- **acceleration** = velocity / time, so units are $\text{m s}^{-2}$
- **force** = mass × acceleration, so units are $\text{kg m s}^{-2}$. The newton is defined by $1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg m s}^{-2}$.
- **energy / work** = force × distance, so units are $\text{kg m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-2}$. The joule is $1\ \text{J} = 1\ \text{kg m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-2}$.
- **power** = energy / time, so units are $\text{kg m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-3}$. The watt is $1\ \text{W} = 1\ \text{kg m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-3}$.
- **pressure / stress** = force / area, so units are $\text{kg m}^{-1}\ \text{s}^{-2}$. The pascal is $1\ \text{Pa} = 1\ \text{kg m}^{-1}\ \text{s}^{-2}$.

When a question asks for the SI base units of a quantity, replace each named unit with its base-unit form and simplify. For example, the SI base units of the watt are $\text{kg m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-3}$.

### Checking the homogeneity of equations

An equation is **homogeneous** when both sides have the same base units. If they differ, the equation is wrong.

Write each side of the equation in base units, then compare. For the equation $v^{2} = u^{2} + 2as$:

- left side: $(\text{m s}^{-1})^{2} = \text{m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-2}$
- right side, first term: $(\text{m s}^{-1})^{2} = \text{m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-2}$
- right side, second term: $\text{m s}^{-2} \cdot \text{m} = \text{m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-2}$

Both sides give $\text{m}^{2}\ \text{s}^{-2}$, so the equation is homogeneous. Homogeneity is necessary but not sufficient — an equation may be homogeneous and still wrong (a wrong constant, a missing factor of 2), but a non-homogeneous equation is definitely wrong.

### Prefixes

You must recall these decimal prefixes and their symbols:

| Prefix | Symbol | Factor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| tera | T | $10^{12}$ |
| giga | G | $10^{9}$ |
| mega | M | $10^{6}$ |
| kilo | k | $10^{3}$ |
| deci | d | $10^{-1}$ |
| centi | c | $10^{-2}$ |
| milli | m | $10^{-3}$ |
| micro | $\mu$ | $10^{-6}$ |
| nano | n | $10^{-9}$ |
| pico | p | $10^{-12}$ |

To convert between prefixed units, replace the prefix with its factor and simplify. For example, $0.25\ \text{kN mm}^{-2}$:

$$
0.25\ \text{kN mm}^{-2} = 0.25 \times \frac{10^{3}\ \text{N}}{(10^{-3}\ \text{m})^{2}} = 0.25 \times \frac{10^{3}}{10^{-6}}\ \text{N m}^{-2} = 2.5 \times 10^{8}\ \text{N m}^{-2}.
$$

Be especially careful with squared units like $\text{mm}^{2}$ — the prefix factor is squared too.

## Errors and uncertainties

Every measurement has an **uncertainty**. The job of an experimenter is to recognise where uncertainties come from, estimate them honestly, and propagate them through to the final answer.

### Systematic and random errors

A **systematic error** shifts every reading by the same amount, in the same direction. Repeating the measurement does not reveal it. Common causes:

- a **zero error** on the instrument (the scale does not read zero when the quantity is zero)
- a **calibration error** (the instrument's scale is wrong)
- parallax, when the observer's eye is consistently to one side of the scale

A systematic error reduces **accuracy** but does not affect precision.

A **random error** scatters readings unpredictably above and below the true value. Causes include observer judgement, fluctuating conditions, and the limited resolution of the instrument. Repeating a measurement many times and taking the mean reduces the effect of random error.

A random error reduces **precision** but, with enough repeats, can leave the mean accurate.

### Precision and accuracy

**Precision** measures how close repeated readings are to each other. A precise set of readings is tightly clustered.

**Accuracy** measures how close a reading (or the mean of repeated readings) is to the true value.

A set of readings can be:

- precise and accurate — tightly clustered around the true value
- precise but not accurate — tightly clustered, but offset from the true value (a systematic error)
- accurate but not precise — scattered widely, but the mean lies near the true value
- neither — scattered and offset

When a question shows a table of repeated readings, compare the **spread** of the data (precision) and the **mean** to the true value (accuracy) separately.

### Uncertainty in derived quantities

A measurement is usually written as $x \pm \Delta x$, where $\Delta x$ is the **absolute uncertainty**. The **percentage uncertainty** is

$$
\text{percentage uncertainty in } x = \frac{\Delta x}{|x|} \times 100\%.
$$

When you combine measured quantities, uncertainties combine in simple ways:

- **Sums and differences** — add the absolute uncertainties.
  If $y = a + b$ or $y = a - b$, then $\Delta y = \Delta a + \Delta b$.
- **Products and quotients** — add the percentage uncertainties.
  If $y = \dfrac{a \cdot b}{c}$, then
  $$
  \frac{\Delta y}{|y|} = \frac{\Delta a}{|a|} + \frac{\Delta b}{|b|} + \frac{\Delta c}{|c|}.
  $$
- **Powers** — multiply the percentage uncertainty by the power.
  If $y = a^{n}$, then $\dfrac{\Delta y}{|y|} = |n| \cdot \dfrac{\Delta a}{|a|}$.

A worked example. The diameter of a ball is measured as $d = (5.26 \pm 0.02)\ \text{cm}$. The volume of a sphere is $V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^{3} = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi (d/2)^{3}$, so $V \propto d^{3}$. The percentage uncertainty in $d$ is

$$
\frac{0.02}{5.26} \times 100\% \approx 0.38\%.
$$

The percentage uncertainty in $V$ is three times this, $\approx 1.14\%$. The value of $V$ is $\tfrac{4}{3}\pi(2.63)^{3} \approx 76.2\ \text{cm}^{3}$, so the absolute uncertainty is $0.0114 \times 76.2 \approx 0.87\ \text{cm}^{3}$. The final answer is $V = (76.2 \pm 0.9)\ \text{cm}^{3}$.

### Significant figures

When you state a derived quantity, give it to the **same number of significant figures** as the least precise measurement that went into it — typically two or three sig figs in this syllabus. An answer with more sig figs than the data justifies is misleading. An answer with fewer throws away precision.

## Scalars and vectors

A **scalar** quantity has magnitude only. A **vector** quantity has both magnitude and direction.

Examples from the syllabus:

- scalars: mass, time, temperature, energy, work, power, distance, speed, pressure, density, electric charge
- vectors: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force (including weight), momentum

A quick test: if it makes sense to ask "in which direction?" the quantity is a vector. "In which direction is the temperature?" makes no sense, so temperature is a scalar. "In which direction is the velocity?" makes sense, so velocity is a vector.

### Adding and subtracting coplanar vectors

To add two coplanar vectors, draw them **tip to tail**. The resultant runs from the tail of the first to the tip of the second.

To subtract $\vec{X} - \vec{Y}$, add the reverse of $\vec{Y}$: $\vec{X} + (-\vec{Y})$. The reverse of $\vec{Y}$ has the same magnitude as $\vec{Y}$ but points the opposite way.

If the two vectors are at right angles, the magnitude of the resultant is

$$
|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{X^{2} + Y^{2}},
$$

and its direction can be found with $\tan\theta = Y / X$.

For two vectors of equal magnitude $F$ with an angle $2\alpha$ between them, the resultant has magnitude $2F\cos\alpha$ along the bisector of the angle.

### Resolving a vector into perpendicular components

Any vector can be split into two **perpendicular components**, usually horizontal and vertical or along and perpendicular to a surface. For a vector $\vec{v}$ at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal:

$$
v_{\text{H}} = v\cos\theta, \qquad v_{\text{V}} = v\sin\theta.
$$

Choose the axes that make the problem simplest. On an inclined plane, resolve the weight into components parallel and perpendicular to the slope:

$$
W_{\parallel} = W\sin\theta, \qquad W_{\perp} = W\cos\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle of the slope to the horizontal.

Resolving is useful whenever you need to find how much of a force (or velocity, or any vector) acts in a particular direction — for example, the component of weight pushing on a trapdoor at an angle, or the horizontal and vertical components of a projectile's velocity just after launch.
