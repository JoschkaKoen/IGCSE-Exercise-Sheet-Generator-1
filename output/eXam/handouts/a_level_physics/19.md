## Capacitance

A **capacitor** stores charge. The simplest example is two parallel conducting plates separated by an insulator (a dielectric, or just vacuum/air). When connected to a battery, charge $+Q$ accumulates on one plate and $-Q$ on the other, and a potential difference $V$ develops across the gap.

The **capacitance** $C$ of any capacitor (or any isolated conductor) is

$$
C = \frac{Q}{V}.
$$

This applies to two kinds of object:

- **Isolated spherical conductor** holding total charge $Q$ at potential $V$ above its surroundings (taken as zero at infinity). For a sphere of radius $r$, $V = Q/(4\pi\varepsilon_{0}r)$, so $C = 4\pi\varepsilon_{0} r$.
- **Parallel-plate capacitor**: charges $\pm Q$ on the two plates and potential difference $V$ between them.

Unit: **farad** (F) $= \text{C V}^{-1}$. The farad is enormous in everyday terms; practical capacitances run from $\text{pF}$ to $\text{mF}$.

Capacitance is a **constant** for a given physical capacitor (set by geometry and dielectric). Doubling the charge on the plates doubles the voltage, leaving $C = Q/V$ unchanged.

### Combining capacitors

Apply $C = Q/V$ together with the connection rules to derive the combinations.

**Capacitors in parallel** all share the same p.d. $V$. The total charge stored is the sum:

$$
Q_{\text{total}} = Q_{1} + Q_{2} + \ldots = C_{1} V + C_{2} V + \ldots
$$

Dividing by $V$:

$$
C_{\text{parallel}} = C_{1} + C_{2} + \ldots
$$

A parallel combination always has **larger** capacitance than any of its components — adding plates in parallel increases the storage capacity.

**Capacitors in series** all carry the **same charge** $Q$ (because the inner plates and connecting wires carry equal and opposite induced charges to keep the net charge there zero). The total p.d. is the sum:

$$
V_{\text{total}} = V_{1} + V_{2} + \ldots = \frac{Q}{C_{1}} + \frac{Q}{C_{2}} + \ldots
$$

Dividing by $Q$:

$$
\frac{1}{C_{\text{series}}} = \frac{1}{C_{1}} + \frac{1}{C_{2}} + \ldots
$$

A series combination always has **smaller** capacitance than any of its components.

Note: the combination rules for capacitors are the **opposite** of those for resistors. (Series sums for resistors; parallel sums for capacitors.) This is because $C = Q/V$ has $V$ in the denominator, while $R = V/I$ has $I$ in the denominator.

## Energy stored in a capacitor

Charging a capacitor from $0$ to $Q$ requires work, because each successive small chunk of charge has to be pushed against the p.d. already established. If the p.d. when the charge is $q$ is $V(q) = q/C$, then bringing up a further small charge $dq$ requires work $V\, dq$.

The total work done is

$$
W = \int_{0}^{Q} \frac{q}{C}\, dq = \frac{Q^{2}}{2 C}.
$$

Equivalently, using $V = Q/C$:

$$
W = \tfrac{1}{2} Q V = \tfrac{1}{2} C V^{2} = \frac{Q^{2}}{2C}.
$$

This is the **energy stored** in the capacitor — recoverable as the capacitor discharges.

### Reading the $Q$ vs $V$ graph

A plot of $Q$ against $V$ for a capacitor is a straight line through the origin with gradient $C$ (since $Q = CV$).

The **energy stored** is the **area under** the $Q$ vs $V$ graph (more precisely, the area between the line and the $V$-axis if you plot $Q$ vertically — be careful which axis is which). For the standard $Q$ vs $V$ plot with $V$ on $x$-axis and $Q$ on $y$-axis, the area under the line up to a given $V$ is $\tfrac{1}{2} Q V$ — the triangle formula.

The factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$ in the energy expression is because the average p.d. during charging is $V/2$ (the p.d. starts at zero and grows linearly to $V$), not $V$.

### Why charging is "half efficient"

If you connect a capacitor of capacitance $C$ to a battery of e.m.f. $V$ (ideal, no internal resistance) through a wire of some small resistance, the capacitor stores energy $\tfrac{1}{2} C V^{2}$. But the battery supplies a charge $Q = CV$ at e.m.f. $V$, so it gives out energy $QV = CV^{2}$. The other half is dissipated as heat in the wire (regardless of the wire's resistance — a counterintuitive but correct result).

## Capacitor discharging through a resistor

Consider a capacitor of capacitance $C$ initially charged to potential $V_{0}$, connected through a switch to a resistor of resistance $R$. When the switch is closed at $t = 0$, current flows and the capacitor discharges.

### Setting up the differential equation (intuition, not required to derive)

Kirchhoff's second law around the loop at any instant during discharge:

$$
V_{C} = V_{R}.
$$

(With no source in the loop, the p.d. across the capacitor must equal the p.d. across the resistor in magnitude — they are connected directly.) Using $V_{C} = Q/C$ and $V_{R} = IR$, and noting $I = -dQ/dt$ (charge decreases as current flows):

$$
\frac{Q}{C} = -R \frac{dQ}{dt}.
$$

This is solved by an **exponential decay** with time constant $RC$.

### Discharge equations

For a capacitor discharging through a resistor, every quantity that decays — charge $Q$, p.d. $V$, current $I$ — does so **exponentially with the same time constant**:

$$
Q = Q_{0} e^{-t / (RC)}, \qquad V = V_{0} e^{-t / (RC)}, \qquad I = I_{0} e^{-t / (RC)}.
$$

Here $Q_{0}$, $V_{0}$, $I_{0}$ are the initial values at $t = 0$ (note $I_{0} = V_{0}/R$).

### Time constant

The quantity

$$
\tau = RC
$$

is the **time constant**. Unit: seconds (verify: $\Omega \cdot \text{F} = (\text{V/A}) \cdot (\text{C/V}) = \text{C/A} = \text{s}$).

Physical meaning: $\tau$ is the time for any decaying quantity to fall to $1/e \approx 0.37$ of its initial value (about 37%). After $2\tau$ it is at $1/e^{2} \approx 0.135$ of initial; after $5\tau$ it is below 1%.

To find $\tau$ from a discharge curve, find the time when the quantity has fallen to $1/e$ of its initial value. Or take logs: $\ln(V/V_{0}) = -t/(RC)$, so plotting $\ln V$ against $t$ gives a straight line with gradient $-1/(RC)$.

### Reading a $Q$-vs-$V$ graph during discharge

For a discharging capacitor, $Q = C V$ at every instant (capacitance is constant). So a plot of $Q$ vs $V_{C}$ during discharge is a **straight line through the origin** with gradient $C$. The discharge moves the operating point from $(V_{0}, Q_{0})$ down the line towards $(0,0)$ over time.

### Reading an $I$-vs-$V$ graph during discharge

For a discharging capacitor, the current in the resistor is $I = V_{R}/R$. Since $V_{R} = V_{C}$ throughout, $I = V_{C}/R$ — a straight line through the origin with gradient $1/R$. So you can find $R$ from the gradient.

### Putting it together — common exam questions

Given a discharge curve $V(t)$ or $Q(t)$ for a specific $RC$ network:

- **Read the initial value** $V_{0}$ or $Q_{0}$ at $t = 0$.
- **Read the time** to fall to $V_{0}/e$ → time constant $\tau = RC$.
- **Given $R$, find $C$** (or vice versa): $C = \tau / R$.
- **Predict the value at a later time** using the exponential formula.
