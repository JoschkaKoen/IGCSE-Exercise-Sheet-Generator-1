## Ultrasound

### Piezo-electric effect

A **piezo-electric crystal** is one that changes shape slightly when an electric p.d. is applied across it, and conversely generates an e.m.f. across itself when its shape is changed (squeezed or stretched). Quartz, lead zirconate titanate (PZT) and barium titanate are common piezo-electric materials.

Two complementary effects in one material:

- **Apply a p.d.** → crystal changes shape (used to generate vibrations).
- **Change the shape** (a wave squeezes the crystal) → an e.m.f. appears (used to detect vibrations).

### Piezo-electric transducer

A **piezo-electric transducer** uses the piezo-electric effect to both **generate** and **detect** ultrasound.

- An alternating p.d. (typically several MHz) is applied across the crystal; the crystal vibrates at the same frequency, sending out longitudinal sound waves at ultrasonic frequencies (above $20\ \text{kHz}$ — typically $1$–$10\ \text{MHz}$ for medical imaging).
- The same crystal is then used as the detector. When reflected ultrasound returns to it, the crystal vibrates and produces an e.m.f. across its faces — read off by the electronics.

A single transducer therefore acts as both emitter and detector, alternating between bursts of transmission and listening intervals.

### Pulse-echo imaging

To obtain diagnostic information about internal structures:

1. The transducer emits a **short pulse** of ultrasound into the body.
2. At each boundary between two tissues (skin to muscle, muscle to bone, etc.), part of the pulse is **reflected back** and part is **transmitted further**.
3. The transducer detects each reflected pulse.
4. The **time delay** between sending and receiving each echo gives the depth of the reflecting boundary: depth $d = c t / 2$ (the pulse travels there and back). The **amplitude** of the echo gives the strength of the reflection.
5. Sweeping the transducer across the body builds up a 2-D image (B-scan).

A **coupling gel** is applied between the transducer and the skin to displace the air. Without it, almost all the ultrasound would reflect at the skin–air boundary (a massive impedance mismatch) and never enter the body.

### Specific acoustic impedance

The **specific acoustic impedance** of a medium is

$$
Z = \rho c,
$$

where $\rho$ is the density and $c$ is the speed of sound in that medium. Unit: $\text{kg m}^{-2}\ \text{s}^{-1}$.

Bone has large $\rho$ and large $c$, so large $Z$. Air has small $\rho$ and modest $c$, so small $Z$. Soft tissues have intermediate values.

### Reflection at a boundary

At a boundary between two media with acoustic impedances $Z_{1}$ and $Z_{2}$, the **intensity reflection coefficient** (fraction of incident intensity that is reflected) is

$$
\frac{I_{\text{R}}}{I_{0}} = \left(\frac{Z_{1} - Z_{2}}{Z_{1} + Z_{2}}\right)^{2}.
$$

- Very different impedances ($Z_{1} \ll Z_{2}$ or vice versa): almost all energy is reflected — too much reflection means nothing penetrates to deeper tissues. (Skin/air is like this — hence the coupling gel.)
- Very similar impedances ($Z_{1} \approx Z_{2}$): hardly any reflection — the boundary cannot be seen by ultrasound, because no detectable echo comes back.
- Best for imaging: impedances are different enough to give a measurable echo, but not so different that nothing passes through.

### Attenuation in matter

As ultrasound travels through tissue, its intensity decreases exponentially with the distance travelled:

$$
I = I_{0} e^{-\mu x},
$$

where $\mu$ is the **linear attenuation coefficient** (unit $\text{m}^{-1}$) for that medium at that frequency.

The same form of equation applies to X-rays — see below.

## X-rays

### Production

X-rays are produced by an **X-ray tube**:

1. A heated cathode emits electrons by thermionic emission.
2. A high p.d. (typically tens to hundreds of kV) accelerates the electrons across a vacuum towards a metal target (the anode; often tungsten).
3. When the electrons strike the metal target, they decelerate sharply. Most of their kinetic energy becomes heat in the target (anodes have cooling), but a small fraction is emitted as X-ray photons (Bremsstrahlung — "braking radiation"). Some electrons knock inner-shell electrons out of the metal atoms, and the refilling of these vacancies emits characteristic X-ray lines.

### Minimum wavelength

The maximum energy a single X-ray photon can have is the full kinetic energy of one accelerated electron — and this occurs when the electron loses all its KE in a single deceleration event. For an accelerating p.d. $V$, the electron's KE is $eV$, so

$$
h f_{\text{max}} = e V, \qquad \lambda_{\text{min}} = \frac{h c}{e V}.
$$

This gives the **cut-off wavelength** at the short end of the X-ray spectrum from a given tube. The continuous Bremsstrahlung spectrum tails off above $\lambda_{\text{min}}$; characteristic lines stick out as sharp peaks at specific wavelengths determined by the target metal.

### Imaging with X-rays

X-rays are sent through the patient onto a detector (originally photographic film, now usually a digital sensor). Tissues that **attenuate** the X-rays more strongly (typically bone, with high $Z$ atoms) cast a stronger "shadow" on the detector — they appear lighter on the printed image; tissues that attenuate less (lungs, soft tissue) cast weaker shadows and appear darker.

The **contrast** in an X-ray image is the **difference in attenuation** between different tissues. High contrast means the image distinguishes tissues clearly; low contrast means similar tissues look alike. Sometimes a contrast medium (e.g. barium meal) is given to enhance soft-tissue contrast.

### Attenuation

The same exponential law as for ultrasound applies:

$$
I = I_{0} e^{-\mu x}.
$$

The linear attenuation coefficient $\mu$ depends on the photon energy and the material. Higher-energy X-rays penetrate further (smaller $\mu$). Bone has a much larger $\mu$ than soft tissue.

To find the **thickness needed to attenuate the beam to a given fraction**: take logs.

$$
x = \frac{1}{\mu} \ln\frac{I_{0}}{I}.
$$

The half-value thickness $x_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \mu$ is the thickness that reduces intensity to half — analogous to half-life in radioactive decay.

### Computed tomography (CT)

A **CT scan** builds a 3-D image of internal structures:

1. The X-ray tube and detector array rotate around the patient, taking many X-ray images of a single thin "slice" of the body from **different angles**.
2. A computer combines these projections (via a mathematical reconstruction algorithm — not required to know the details) into a single **2-D cross-section** of the slice.
3. The patient (or the gantry) is moved along the long axis of the body so the next adjacent slice can be imaged, and the process repeats.
4. The stack of 2-D slices is combined into a **3-D image** of the body region.

CT gives much more detail than a single X-ray, because soft tissues that overlap in a single projection are separated by the multiple-angle reconstruction.

## PET scanning

### Tracer

A **tracer** is a substance containing radioactive nuclei that is introduced into the body (injection, ingestion, or inhalation). It is preferentially taken up by the tissue being studied (e.g. tumour tissue absorbs more glucose-tagged tracer because of its high metabolism). The radioactive decay can then be detected from outside the body.

In **positron emission tomography (PET) scanning**, the tracer is a $\beta^{+}$ emitter — a nuclide that decays by emitting a positron. A common choice is fluorine-18 attached to a glucose analogue (FDG).

### Annihilation

When a particle meets its **antiparticle** they **annihilate**: their total mass is converted into electromagnetic energy. In PET:

- A positron from the tracer decay travels only a few mm in tissue before encountering an electron.
- The electron and positron annihilate. Mass-energy is conserved: the total rest mass energy is converted into electromagnetic photon energy. Momentum is also conserved.
- Because the initial total momentum is approximately zero (both particles nearly at rest after the positron has slowed down), the products must have zero total momentum. Two photons are produced, each carrying $511\ \text{keV}$ of energy ($= m_{e} c^{2}$ each), travelling in **exactly opposite directions** (so their momenta cancel).

### Energy of the annihilation gamma photons

By energy conservation, the total photon energy equals the total mass-energy of the electron-positron pair:

$$
2 h f = 2 m_{e} c^{2}, \qquad h f = m_{e} c^{2}.
$$

Each photon has $h f = m_{e} c^{2} = (9.11 \times 10^{-31})(3.00 \times 10^{8})^{2} = 8.2 \times 10^{-14}\ \text{J} \approx 0.51\ \text{MeV}$. Wavelength: $\lambda = h/(m_{e} c) \approx 2.4 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{m}$.

(If the electron and positron have non-negligible kinetic energies, the photon energies are slightly higher than this, but in PET the positron has slowed to a stop in the tissue, so the calculation uses just the rest masses.)

### Reconstructing the image

The two annihilation photons emerge from the body in opposite directions and are detected by **detector rings** surrounding the patient.

By recording the **two simultaneous arrivals** at opposite detectors (a "coincidence"), the system identifies the line on which the annihilation occurred. By collecting many coincidences from many angles, the computer reconstructs a **3-D map of the tracer concentration** within the body — highlighting tissues with high metabolic activity (the tumour, for example).

The **arrival times** of the two photons at the two detectors can also be compared. Since both photons travel at $c$ and start at the same point, a small time difference indicates that one detector is slightly closer to the emission point — this can be used to refine the location along the line of response (time-of-flight PET).
