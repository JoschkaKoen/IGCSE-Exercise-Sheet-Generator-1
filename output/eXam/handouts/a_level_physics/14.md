## Thermal equilibrium

Heat (thermal energy) flows from a region of **higher temperature to a region of lower temperature**. When two bodies are placed in contact, energy is transferred until their temperatures are equal — they have reached **thermal equilibrium**. At equilibrium there is no net flow of energy.

Two regions at the **same temperature** are in thermal equilibrium with each other. There is no net flow of energy between them, even though microscopic exchanges still occur.

Temperature is the property that determines the direction of heat flow. It is not a measure of heat content. A small cup of boiling water (100 °C) holds far less thermal energy than a swimming pool at 25 °C, but a piece of metal placed in the cup would gain energy while one placed in the pool would lose it.

## Measuring temperature: physical properties that vary with temperature

Any physical property that **changes reproducibly with temperature** can be used to make a thermometer. Examples in the syllabus:

- **Density of a liquid** — used in a liquid-in-glass thermometer (e.g. mercury or alcohol). As temperature rises, the liquid expands and its level rises in a narrow capillary.
- **Volume of a gas at constant pressure** — used in a constant-pressure gas thermometer. Gas volume rises linearly with thermodynamic temperature.
- **Resistance of a metal** — used in a resistance thermometer. The resistance of a metal increases approximately linearly with temperature over a wide range.
- **E.m.f. of a thermocouple** — used to measure temperatures over a very wide range. Two different metals joined at two junctions; the e.m.f. produced depends on the temperature difference between the junctions.

Different thermometers may give slightly different readings if the property's variation is non-linear; the readings agree only at the calibration points.

## Thermodynamic temperature scale

The **thermodynamic temperature** (or absolute temperature) scale is defined **without reference to any particular substance** — it depends only on the laws of thermodynamics. Its unit is the **kelvin** (K).

### Absolute zero

The lowest possible temperature is **zero kelvin** ($0\ \text{K}$), known as **absolute zero**. At absolute zero a system has its minimum possible internal energy — particles have no random translational motion (and very little energy of any kind). No body can be cooled below this temperature.

### Celsius scale

The Celsius scale, $\theta$, is offset from the thermodynamic scale by a fixed amount:

$$
T / \text{K} = \theta / {}^{\circ}\text{C} + 273.15.
$$

So $0\ ^{\circ}\text{C} = 273.15\ \text{K}$ (the freezing point of pure water at atmospheric pressure), and $100\ ^{\circ}\text{C} = 373.15\ \text{K}$ (the boiling point). The size of the kelvin equals the size of the degree Celsius — a temperature **difference** of $1\ \text{K}$ is the same as $1\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$, but absolute temperatures differ by $273.15$.

In gas-law calculations (PV = nRT etc.), you must always use **absolute** temperatures in kelvin. Using °C will give wrong answers.

## Specific heat capacity

The **specific heat capacity** $c$ of a substance is the energy required to raise the temperature of **unit mass** by **one kelvin** (or equivalently, one degree Celsius — temperature **differences** are the same in both scales).

$$
c = \frac{Q}{m \Delta T} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad Q = m c \Delta T.
$$

Unit: $\text{J kg}^{-1}\ \text{K}^{-1}$.

Examples:

- water: $c \approx 4200\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\ \text{K}^{-1}$ (high, which is why water is a good coolant and why oceans moderate climate).
- aluminium: $c \approx 900\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\ \text{K}^{-1}$.
- copper: $c \approx 385\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\ \text{K}^{-1}$.

To find an unknown specific heat experimentally: supply known energy $Q$ (electrically, via $Q = VIt$, measuring the power input), measure the resulting temperature rise $\Delta T$ in a sample of known mass $m$. Then $c = Q/(m\Delta T)$. Reduce heat loss with insulation and by using a temperature rise of about 10 K (large enough to measure accurately, small enough to limit losses).

When two bodies at different temperatures reach thermal equilibrium and no heat is lost to the surroundings, the energy gained by the colder body equals the energy lost by the hotter body:

$$
m_{1} c_{1} (T_{\text{eq}} - T_{1}) = m_{2} c_{2} (T_{2} - T_{\text{eq}}).
$$

Solve for $T_{\text{eq}}$ or whichever unknown is needed.

## Specific latent heat

When a substance changes phase (solid ↔ liquid, or liquid ↔ gas) at constant temperature, energy must be supplied (or removed) without any temperature change. This energy is the **latent heat**.

The **specific latent heat** $L$ is the energy required to change the phase of **unit mass** of substance at constant temperature:

$$
L = \frac{Q}{m} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad Q = m L.
$$

Unit: $\text{J kg}^{-1}$.

Two kinds:

- **Specific latent heat of fusion** $L_{\text{f}}$ — for melting or freezing (solid ↔ liquid).
- **Specific latent heat of vaporisation** $L_{\text{v}}$ — for boiling or condensing (liquid ↔ gas).

Both refer to a fixed temperature (the melting or boiling temperature at the given pressure).

For water at atmospheric pressure: $L_{\text{f}} \approx 3.34 \times 10^{5}\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$ (at $0\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$); $L_{\text{v}} \approx 2.26 \times 10^{6}\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$ (at $100\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$). Note $L_{\text{v}}$ is about 7 times $L_{\text{f}}$.

### Why $L_{\text{v}} > L_{\text{f}}$

Two reasons combine, both following from the molecular picture of matter:

1. **Bonds**: in fusion, only some of the bonds between molecules are broken; the molecules remain close together as a liquid. In vaporisation, **all** intermolecular bonds must be broken so that molecules can separate completely. More energy is needed to break all the bonds.
2. **Work done against atmospheric pressure**: when a liquid becomes a gas it expands by a huge factor (water vapour has roughly $10^{3}$ times the volume of the liquid). The substance does work pushing back the surrounding atmosphere. This work is supplied from the energy input.

The energy in fusion goes mostly into breaking some bonds; the energy in vaporisation goes into breaking all the remaining bonds plus doing expansion work — so $L_{\text{v}}$ is larger.

### Solving multi-step problems

If a problem mixes temperature change and phase change (e.g. ice at $-5\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$ warming up to water at $30\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$):

1. **Sensible heating** of the solid from $-5\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$ to $0\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$: $Q_{1} = m c_{\text{ice}} \times 5$.
2. **Melting** at $0\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$: $Q_{2} = m L_{\text{f}}$.
3. **Sensible heating** of the water from $0\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$ to $30\ ^{\circ}\text{C}$: $Q_{3} = m c_{\text{water}} \times 30$.

Total energy required: $Q_{1} + Q_{2} + Q_{3}$. Phase changes happen at constant temperature, so the "$\Delta T$" during melting is zero — use $mL$, not $mc\Delta T$.

When a question gives a heater power $P$ and asks for the time taken, use $Q = Pt$ (assuming no heat loss). Reducing heat loss to the surroundings, lagging the container, and using a small mass of substance are common improvements to the experiment.
