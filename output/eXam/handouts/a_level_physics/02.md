## Definitions

These five quantities recur in every kinematics question. Learn the definitions exactly — examiners reward precise wording.

- **Distance** — the total length of path travelled. Scalar.
- **Displacement** — the straight-line distance from the start point to the end point, with a direction. Vector.
- **Speed** — the rate of change of distance with time. Scalar.
- **Velocity** — the rate of change of displacement with time. Vector.
- **Acceleration** — the rate of change of velocity with time. Vector.

The unit of speed and velocity is $\text{m s}^{-1}$; the unit of acceleration is $\text{m s}^{-2}$.

A common pitfall: "deceleration" is just acceleration with the opposite sign to the velocity. There is no separate quantity.

## Motion graphs

Many marks come from reading or sketching motion graphs. Two graphs matter:

### Displacement–time graph

The **gradient** of a displacement–time graph at a point gives the **velocity** at that instant.

- Horizontal line → object at rest.
- Straight, sloping line → constant velocity (gradient = velocity).
- Curved line → changing velocity. Draw a tangent at the point of interest and find its gradient.

### Velocity–time graph

The **gradient** of a velocity–time graph at a point gives the **acceleration** at that instant.

The **area between the line and the time axis** gives the **displacement** during that interval.

- Horizontal line → constant velocity (zero acceleration).
- Straight, sloping line → uniform acceleration.
- Curved line → changing acceleration.
- Area above the time axis is positive displacement; area below is negative (object moved backwards).

To find displacement, split the area into triangles and rectangles, or count grid squares. Use the formulas $\tfrac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$ for a triangle and $\text{base} \times \text{height}$ for a rectangle.

## The four SUVAT equations

For motion in a straight line with **uniform acceleration**, five symbols are used: initial velocity $u$, final velocity $v$, acceleration $a$, displacement $s$, and time $t$. Four equations connect them:

$$
v = u + at
$$
$$
s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2} a t^{2}
$$
$$
s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u + v)t
$$
$$
v^{2} = u^{2} + 2as
$$

Each equation involves four of the five symbols. To choose the right one, list what you know and what you want, then pick the equation that contains exactly those four.

### Deriving SUVAT from a velocity–time graph

You should be able to derive these from the definitions of velocity and acceleration:

- $v = u + at$ comes from $a = (v - u)/t$, the **gradient** of the line.
- $s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u + v) t$ is the **area** under the line — a trapezium with parallel sides $u$ and $v$ and width $t$.
- $s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^{2}$ comes from substituting $v = u + at$ into the area expression.
- $v^{2} = u^{2} + 2as$ comes from eliminating $t$ between the first two.

A question that asks "which equation can be derived using only the gradient of a velocity–time graph?" wants $v = u + at$ (since the gradient itself defines acceleration).

### Sign convention

Pick a positive direction at the start of the problem and stick with it. Quantities pointing the other way carry a minus sign. For a ball thrown upwards, if you take "up" as positive, then $u$ is positive, $a = -g$ (gravity acts downwards), and the ball's displacement at the highest point is positive while its velocity there is zero.

## Free fall under gravity

When **air resistance is negligible**, an object in free fall has constant acceleration $g \approx 9.81\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ downwards, regardless of its mass.

For a ball released from rest and falling a distance $h$:

$$
h = \tfrac{1}{2}gt^{2}, \qquad v = gt, \qquad v^{2} = 2gh.
$$

For a ball thrown vertically upwards with speed $u$:

- maximum height reached: set $v = 0$ in $v^{2} = u^{2} - 2gh$, giving $h = u^{2}/(2g)$.
- time to reach maximum height: set $v = 0$ in $v = u - gt$, giving $t = u/g$.
- total time of flight back to launch height: $2u/g$ (symmetric).

### Experiment to determine $g$

A standard method: release an object from rest, measure the distance $h$ it falls and the time $t$ taken. Then

$$
g = \frac{2h}{t^{2}}.
$$

Repeat for several heights and plot $h$ against $t^{2}$. The gradient of the best-fit line is $g/2$, so $g$ is twice the gradient. Repeating reduces random error; an electronic timer (light gates or a trapdoor circuit) avoids reaction-time error.

## Two-dimensional motion: independence of components

When motion is uniform in one direction (e.g. horizontal) and uniformly accelerating in a **perpendicular** direction (e.g. vertical under gravity), the two components are **independent**. Analyse each direction separately with its own SUVAT equation.

### Horizontal projection

An object launched horizontally with speed $u_{\text{H}}$ from height $h$, with air resistance negligible:

- **Horizontal**: constant velocity $u_{\text{H}}$. After time $t$, horizontal distance $x = u_{\text{H}} t$.
- **Vertical**: starts from rest, accelerates downwards at $g$. After time $t$, vertical drop $y = \tfrac{1}{2} g t^{2}$ and vertical velocity $v_{\text{V}} = g t$.

The time to hit the ground depends **only** on the height $h$, not on $u_{\text{H}}$. Set $h = \tfrac{1}{2} g t^{2}$ to find $t$; then horizontal range is $u_{\text{H}} t$.

The horizontal-velocity graph is a horizontal line at $u_{\text{H}}$. The vertical-velocity graph is a straight line through the origin with gradient $g$.

### Projectile at an angle

A projectile launched at speed $u$ at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal:

- horizontal component of initial velocity: $u_{\text{H}} = u \cos\theta$ (constant throughout flight).
- vertical component of initial velocity: $u_{\text{V}} = u \sin\theta$ (decreases, becomes zero at the top, then increases downwards).

At the highest point, $v_{\text{V}} = 0$ but $v_{\text{H}}$ still equals $u\cos\theta$. The time to the highest point is $t_{\text{up}} = u\sin\theta / g$; the total time of flight (returning to launch height) is $2t_{\text{up}}$.

### Bouncing ball

When a ball bounces, the velocity–time graph shows straight sloping segments (constant $g$) with sudden jumps at each bounce (velocity reverses sign and is reduced in magnitude if some energy is lost). Total time and total distance fallen accumulate across bounces.

## Catching-up problems

When two objects move along the same line with different motions, write a displacement equation for each, using the same time origin and positive direction, then set the displacements equal (or set the difference to a given gap).

For a goods train moving at constant velocity $u_{\text{G}}$ and an express train accelerating from rest at $a$, both starting at the same point at $t = 0$:

$$
s_{\text{G}} = u_{\text{G}} t, \qquad s_{\text{E}} = \tfrac{1}{2} a t^{2}.
$$

They meet again when $s_{\text{G}} = s_{\text{E}}$, giving $t = 2 u_{\text{G}} / a$.

## Tips for problem-solving

1. **Draw a diagram** and label the positive direction.
2. **List the SUVAT symbols** with their known and unknown values, including signs.
3. **Pick the SUVAT equation** that contains exactly the four symbols you have plus the one you want.
4. For projectile motion, **split into horizontal and vertical** SUVAT problems linked only by the time $t$.
5. Always check units in your final answer and that the magnitude is reasonable.
