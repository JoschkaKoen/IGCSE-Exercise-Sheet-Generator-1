## Simple harmonic motion: definition

A particle oscillates with **simple harmonic motion** (SHM) when its **acceleration is**:

- **proportional to its displacement** from a fixed equilibrium point, and
- **directed back towards that point** — opposite in sign to the displacement.

The defining equation is

$$
a = -\omega^{2} x,
$$

where $x$ is displacement from equilibrium and $\omega$ is a positive constant called the **angular frequency**. The minus sign captures "directed back towards equilibrium".

Many physical systems obey this near a stable equilibrium: a mass on a spring, a pendulum (small-angle), a floating block displaced vertically, charge on a capacitor in an LC circuit, atoms oscillating in a solid.

### Key terms for oscillations

- **Displacement** $x$ — distance from equilibrium at a given instant (vector along the line of oscillation).
- **Amplitude** $x_{0}$ — the maximum displacement from equilibrium. Always positive.
- **Period** $T$ — time for one complete oscillation.
- **Frequency** $f$ — number of complete oscillations per second; $f = 1/T$. Unit: Hz.
- **Angular frequency** $\omega$ — $\omega = 2\pi/T = 2\pi f$. Unit: $\text{rad s}^{-1}$.
- **Phase difference** — fraction of a cycle (expressed in radians) by which one oscillation leads or lags another. Two oscillators a quarter cycle apart have phase difference $\pi/2$.

So you can convert: $T = 2\pi/\omega$, $f = \omega/(2\pi)$. Given any one of $\omega$, $f$ or $T$ you can find the others.

## Displacement, velocity, acceleration in SHM

If the oscillator starts at $x = 0$ moving in the positive direction at $t = 0$, its displacement is

$$
x = x_{0} \sin (\omega t).
$$

Differentiating once gives the velocity:

$$
v = \frac{dx}{dt} = x_{0} \omega \cos(\omega t) = v_{0} \cos(\omega t),
$$

where $v_{0} = x_{0} \omega$ is the **maximum speed** (which occurs as the particle passes through equilibrium).

Differentiating again gives the acceleration:

$$
a = \frac{dv}{dt} = -x_{0} \omega^{2} \sin(\omega t) = -\omega^{2} x.
$$

This recovers the SHM defining equation.

(If the oscillator starts at the extreme position $x = x_{0}$ at $t = 0$, use $x = x_{0} \cos(\omega t)$ instead. Both are valid solutions; choose whichever fits the initial conditions.)

### Velocity in terms of displacement

A useful relation, independent of time:

$$
v = \pm \omega \sqrt{x_{0}^{2} - x^{2}}.
$$

- At equilibrium $x = 0$: $v = \pm \omega x_{0}$ (maximum speed). Both signs because the particle passes through equilibrium twice per cycle — once each way.
- At extreme $x = \pm x_{0}$: $v = 0$ (instantaneously at rest).

You can derive this from energy conservation (next section), or by combining $x = x_{0}\sin\omega t$ and $v = x_{0}\omega \cos\omega t$ and eliminating $t$ via $\sin^{2} + \cos^{2} = 1$.

### Graphs of $x$, $v$, $a$ against time

For $x = x_{0}\sin\omega t$:

- $x$ vs $t$ — sine curve, amplitude $x_{0}$, period $T = 2\pi/\omega$.
- $v$ vs $t$ — cosine curve, **leading $x$ by $\pi/2$**, amplitude $\omega x_{0}$.
- $a$ vs $t$ — negative sine curve, **out of phase with $x$ by $\pi$** (180°), amplitude $\omega^{2} x_{0}$.

### Graph of $a$ against $x$

A straight line through the origin with **negative gradient** $-\omega^{2}$. So $\omega$ can be read from a graph: gradient $= -\omega^{2}$, so $\omega = \sqrt{|\text{gradient}|}$. From there, $T = 2\pi / \omega$ and $f = 1/T$.

This is a common exam pattern: a graph of $a$ vs $x$ (or $a$ vs $h$ for a floating block) lets you deduce $\omega$ from the gradient.

## Energy in simple harmonic motion

A simple harmonic oscillator continuously exchanges energy between two forms:

- **Kinetic energy** $E_{\text{K}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}$.
- **Potential energy** $E_{\text{P}}$ (elastic for a spring, gravitational for a pendulum, etc.).

In the absence of damping, the **total energy** is constant.

### Maximum and minimum

- At the **equilibrium position** ($x = 0$): $v$ is maximum, so $E_{\text{K}}$ is maximum and $E_{\text{P}}$ is minimum (zero, by choice of zero).
- At the **extreme positions** ($x = \pm x_{0}$): $v = 0$, so $E_{\text{K}} = 0$ and $E_{\text{P}}$ is maximum.

The two interchange continuously: as the particle moves from extreme to equilibrium, $E_{\text{P}}$ falls and $E_{\text{K}}$ rises by the same amount.

### Total energy

Using $v_{\text{max}} = \omega x_{0}$:

$$
E_{\text{total}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v_{\text{max}}^{2} = \tfrac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x_{0}^{2}.
$$

Two key facts:

- **Total energy is proportional to the square of the amplitude.** Doubling the amplitude quadruples the total energy.
- **Total energy is proportional to $\omega^{2}$**, i.e. to the square of the frequency.

### Energy as a function of displacement

Using $v^{2} = \omega^{2}(x_{0}^{2} - x^{2})$,

$$
E_{\text{K}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} (x_{0}^{2} - x^{2}), \qquad E_{\text{P}} = \tfrac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x^{2}.
$$

So $E_{\text{K}}$ is a **downward parabola** with peak $\tfrac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x_{0}^{2}$ at $x = 0$, falling to zero at $x = \pm x_{0}$. $E_{\text{P}}$ is an **upward parabola** with minimum at $x = 0$ and maximum at $x = \pm x_{0}$. Their sum is constant.

A graph of $E_{\text{K}}$ vs $x$ is parabolic, with the points where it touches the $x$-axis at $\pm x_{0}$ giving the amplitude directly.

## Damped oscillations

A **resistive force** (friction, drag, air resistance) on an oscillator causes **damping** — the amplitude decreases over time as energy is dissipated as heat. Three regimes are named:

### Light damping

Amplitude **decreases gradually** (typically exponentially) over many cycles. The system still oscillates at approximately its natural frequency, but each cycle has a smaller amplitude than the last. A car suspension is engineered to be light to medium damping so that bumps don't shake the car indefinitely but you still feel a smooth motion.

### Critical damping

The smallest amount of damping for which the displaced system returns to equilibrium **without overshooting and without oscillating**. The system reaches equilibrium in the shortest possible time. Galvanometers and analogue voltmeters are designed for critical damping so their needles settle quickly.

### Heavy (over) damping

So much resistive force that the system returns slowly to equilibrium, again without oscillating, but more slowly than the critical case. A door fitted with a strong hydraulic closer is heavily damped.

Sketches: on a displacement-vs-time graph, light damping shows a sinusoid whose envelope decays exponentially; critical damping shows a single exponential curve returning quickly to zero with no overshoot; heavy damping shows a slower exponential return.

## Forced oscillations and resonance

A **forced oscillator** is one driven by an external periodic force at a frequency $f_{\text{d}}$ chosen by the experimenter. The system responds by oscillating at the **driving frequency** $f_{\text{d}}$ (not its own natural frequency).

The amplitude of the response depends on $f_{\text{d}}$. Plotting the amplitude against $f_{\text{d}}$ gives a **resonance curve** with a peak.

### Resonance

**Resonance** is the condition where the driving frequency equals the **natural frequency** $f_{0}$ of the system. At resonance:

- the amplitude of the oscillation reaches a **maximum**,
- energy transfer from the driver to the oscillator is most efficient.

Examples:

- a swing pushed at the right rate (its natural frequency) builds up to a large amplitude.
- a wine glass shattered by a sound whose frequency matches its natural ringing frequency.
- a building shaken by an earthquake whose seismic-wave frequency matches a structural natural frequency — engineers deliberately design buildings so their natural frequencies fall outside the dominant seismic band.

The shape of the resonance peak depends on damping: **lighter damping → sharper, higher peak**; **heavier damping → broader, lower peak**, with the peak slightly shifted to lower frequency. Critical and heavy damping suppress resonance entirely.
