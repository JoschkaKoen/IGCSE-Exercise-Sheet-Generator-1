## Ethics for computing professionals

A **computing professional** is someone whose work — writing software, designing systems, managing networks, storing data — affects other people. Because the work is technical, others usually cannot easily judge whether it has been done well or honestly. The profession has therefore built up a set of **ethical principles** to guide behaviour.

### Why ethics matters

Two reasons:

- **Trust** — users and employers trust that professionals act in the user's interest. Without ethics, that trust collapses, and software (and the profession) loses credibility.
- **Impact** — software runs critical infrastructure: medical devices, banking systems, vehicles, communication. Sloppy or dishonest work can hurt people.

Professional bodies (e.g. BCS, ACM, IEEE) publish **codes of ethics** that members are expected to follow.

### Typical ethical principles

- **Public interest first** — protect the safety, health and welfare of the people affected by your work.
- **Honesty and competence** — be honest about your skills, your work and any limitations; don't claim expertise you don't have; don't take on work you can't deliver.
- **Confidentiality** — protect the confidential information of clients and employers.
- **Avoid conflicts of interest** — don't take work where your personal interest conflicts with the client's.
- **Continuing professional development** — keep your skills up to date.
- **Respect intellectual property** — don't pirate code or breach copyright.
- **Respect privacy** — handle personal data responsibly.
- **Treat colleagues fairly** — give credit, avoid discrimination, support junior colleagues.

### Impact of acting ethically vs unethically

**Acting ethically**:

- Users are protected from harm.
- The reputation of the professional, the team and the wider profession is strengthened.
- Legal and regulatory exposure is reduced.
- Trust accumulates — clients return, recommend the professional to others.

**Acting unethically** (cutting corners on testing, hiding bugs, misusing data, taking shortcuts on security):

- Real users can be harmed (a faulty product, an exposed personal record, a financial loss).
- The professional may face dismissal, legal action, or loss of licence.
- The reputation of the team or company is damaged.
- Trust in the profession overall erodes — users become wary of technology in general.

### Applying ethics in practice

When you face a borderline decision (a deadline pressure, a request from a manager, an opportunity for unauthorised use of data):

1. **Identify whose interests are affected** — users, colleagues, employer, the public.
2. **Check the relevant code of ethics** and any laws / regulations.
3. **Consider the consequences** of each option.
4. **Talk to a trusted senior** if the situation is unclear.
5. **Choose the option that protects users and the public** above short-term convenience.

## Copyright

**Copyright** is the legal right that the creator of an original work has to control how it is **copied, distributed, modified, and performed**. It applies automatically — no registration needed — to original works expressed in a tangible form, including:

- source code,
- compiled software,
- documents and books,
- images, audio, video,
- creative works generally.

### Need for copyright legislation

Without copyright:

- Anyone could copy software and distribute it freely; the original developer would not be paid for their work, reducing the incentive to make software.
- Users could not be confident about which version they have, or hold the original author accountable.
- Plagiarism would be effectively legal.

With copyright:

- Developers can sell their work and earn a living, encouraging more software to be produced.
- Users know who created the software and can hold them to account.
- Re-use and adaptation can happen on the developer's terms, through licensing.

Copyright lasts for a long time (e.g. 70 years after the creator's death in many countries). Some elements (like algorithms or general ideas) are not covered by copyright but may be covered by **patents** (a separate legal regime).

## Software licences

A **software licence** is a legal contract that grants permission to use the software, on terms set by the copyright owner. Different licence types are used in different situations.

### Commercial (proprietary) licence

- The user **buys a licence** (one-off or subscription) and may use the software only within its terms (often on one machine, by one user, for some non-commercial or commercial purpose).
- **Source code is not provided**; the user can't modify the program.
- The licence forbids **further distribution** of the software (you cannot give copies to friends).
- Pirated copies are illegal; the licence usually carries a unique product key or online activation.
- Examples: Microsoft Office (Microsoft 365), Adobe Photoshop, most games.

Use a commercial licence when the developer wants to **earn revenue** from each user and **retain control** of the code.

### Open-source licence

- Source code is **made publicly available**; users can read, modify and redistribute it.
- Different open-source licences impose different conditions:
  - **Permissive** (e.g. MIT, BSD) — almost unrestricted use; you can include the code in your own commercial product as long as you keep the copyright notice.
  - **Copyleft** (e.g. GPL) — anyone who distributes modified versions must also release the changes under the same open-source licence ("share-alike").
- Examples: Linux kernel (GPL), Python (PSF licence), Apache web server (Apache licence), most web frameworks.

Use an open-source licence when the developer wants the software **widely used and improved by the community**, and is willing to give up direct revenue from sale.

### Freeware

- **Free of charge** but the **source code is not made available**.
- Users may use and redistribute the software freely, but not modify it.
- The developer often retains all rights other than the right to use it.
- Sometimes funded by advertising or by an upgrade path to a paid "pro" version.
- Examples: Adobe Acrobat Reader, Skype, WhatsApp.

Use freeware when you want **wide distribution** without releasing the source — perhaps to support a paid product, or as a public-good utility.

### Shareware

- Distributed **free of charge for a trial period**, then the user must pay to continue using it (or features are limited until paid).
- Source code is not provided.
- Encourages users to try before they buy.
- Examples: some shareware utilities, trial versions of commercial games.

Use shareware when you want users to **experience the product** before purchasing.

### Comparing the licence types

| Type | Cost | Source | Redistribute | Modify |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Commercial | Paid | No | No | No |
| Open-source | Free | Yes | Yes | Yes (often with conditions) |
| Freeware | Free | No | Yes | No |
| Shareware | Free trial, then paid | No | Sometimes | No |

When asked to justify the use of a particular licence, link the choice to:

- the developer's goal (revenue, distribution, community involvement),
- the user's needs (cost, customisability),
- legal compliance,
- the practical use case.

## Artificial Intelligence (AI)

**Artificial Intelligence** is the area of computer science concerned with building systems that perform tasks that have traditionally been thought of as requiring **human intelligence** — recognising speech and images, translating language, playing complex games, driving vehicles, making decisions from data.

Modern AI is built mostly from **machine learning** — algorithms that improve their performance at a task by learning patterns from large amounts of data, rather than being explicitly programmed step by step. **Deep learning**, using artificial neural networks with many layers, is the dominant approach today.

### Examples in everyday computing

- **Speech recognition** — converting spoken words to text (voice assistants, dictation, hands-free interfaces).
- **Image recognition** — identifying objects, faces or text in images; used in photo apps, accessibility tools, medical scans, self-checkout supermarkets.
- **Machine translation** — automated translation between languages.
- **Recommendation systems** — suggesting products, videos or music based on user history.
- **Natural-language interfaces** — chatbots, conversational search.
- **Autonomous vehicles and robots** — perception, planning, control.

### A common scenario in exams

A program reads text on a label using a digital camera, translates it, and reads it out as audio. This uses AI for:

- **Optical character recognition** to find words in the camera image.
- **Machine translation** to convert words from one language to another.
- **Text-to-speech** to produce natural-sounding audio output.

### Benefits of AI

- **Accessibility** — speech and image-recognition AI help users with visual or hearing impairments; translation helps non-native speakers and tourists; voice control helps those with limited mobility.
- **Productivity** — automating repetitive tasks (data entry, image sorting, language translation) frees humans for more creative work.
- **Decision support** — AI can spot patterns in huge datasets that humans would miss (medical diagnosis, fraud detection, weather prediction).
- **Always available** — AI doesn't need breaks, runs 24/7, can serve many users in parallel.
- **Personalisation** — AI can tailor content and services to each user's preferences and history.

### Concerns about AI

- **Bias** — AI learns from training data, so unfair patterns in the data become unfair patterns in the AI's decisions (hiring, lending, sentencing).
- **Job displacement** — AI may replace human workers in some roles, requiring retraining.
- **Privacy** — AI training often consumes large amounts of personal data.
- **Transparency** — large AI models are "black boxes"; it can be hard to explain why a particular decision was made.
- **Accountability** — when AI makes a decision that turns out to be wrong (a misdiagnosis, an accident), who is responsible — the developer, the user, the operator?
- **Misuse** — AI can also be used for deepfakes, automated misinformation, surveillance.

Computing professionals are ethically obliged to **understand the limits** of AI systems they build or deploy, **inform users** about them, and **mitigate harm**.
