## Mass and Inertia

### Key formulas
- $F = ma$ (Newton's second law)
- $p = mv$ (Momentum definition)
- $m = \frac{F}{a}$ (Inertial mass definition)
- $W = mg$ (Weight definition)

### Core idea
When you push or pull an object, you change its **speed** or **direction**, but you do not change its **mass**. Mass measures the amount of **matter** inside the object, and it stays the same unless you add or remove material. Force creates **acceleration**, which changes how fast the object moves, but the **inertia** stays fixed. You can think of mass as a built-in **resistance** to motion that never shifts under normal conditions.

### Worked micro-example
1. A cart has a mass of $4.0\text{ kg}$ and moves at $2.0\text{ m/s}$.
2. You apply a constant force of $8.0\text{ N}$ to the cart for $3.0\text{ s}$.
3. Calculate the acceleration using $a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{8.0}{4.0} = 2.0\text{ m/s}^2$.
4. Find the new velocity: $v = v_0 + at = 2.0 + (2.0 \times 3.0) = 8.0\text{ m/s}$.
5. Check the mass after the force stops acting.

**Result: 4.0 kg**

### Common pitfalls
- Do not confuse **mass** with **weight**, because weight changes when gravity changes.
- Remember that **force** changes **velocity**, not the amount of **matter** in the object.
- In high-speed physics, **relativistic mass** is an outdated concept; modern exams use **rest mass** only.
